    Briefing: Analysis of the Panel “Challenges for the Maintenance of the Standing Forest” — COP30

    COP30 — Panel reveals Land Neocolonialism in Brazil

    The panel "Challenges for the Maintenance of the Standing Forest," held at COP30 in Belém, revealed a profound dichotomy that defines the climate debate in Brazil. On one side, robust scientific and economic advances were presented that enable the valuation and financing of the standing forest. On the other hand, critical failures in land governance and market regulation were exposed, which not only undermine these advances but also transform market instruments into vectors of socio-environmental conflicts.

    Main Advances Presented:

    • Quantification Science: A new carbon measurement methodology (UNESP/University of Sassari), published in Scientific Reports, demonstrated that IPCC standards underestimate carbon in tropical soil by up to 90%, providing a more accurate scientific basis for valuing forest assets.
    • Economic Paradigm: The concept of "sociobiodiversity bioeconomy" (UFPA/NAEA) was presented—an economic model that values diversity management and traditional knowledge, in direct opposition to industrial homogenization, and whose GDP in Pará is estimated at R$ 7 billion.
    • Agro-Forest Interdependence: The thesis that forest maintenance is essential for the resilience and carbon neutrality of agriculture was defended, with "soil health" as a central pillar.

    Main Failures and Threats Identified:

    • Systemic Land Weakness: The core problem is Brazil's "profound land weakness," exemplified by the 50 million hectares of unallocated public lands in the Amazon, which serve as a stage for land grabbing (grilagem).
    • "Agrarian Metaverse" and "Carbon Neocolonialism": The Rural Environmental Registry (CAR), being self-declaratory, is instrumentalized to create a virtual land reality. This "agrarian metaverse" allows companies to generate carbon credits on the territories of traditional communities without consultation or consent, configuring a "carbon neocolonialism" and "green grabbing," with documented cases in Breves and Portel (PA).
    • Problematic Market Structures: Pará State's agreement with the LEAF Coalition was criticized for the undervalued carbon price (US 15/ton against an estimated Social Cost of Carbon of US 185/ton) and for corporate structures that raise sovereignty issues.

    Core Conclusion: The primary challenge for maintaining the standing forest is not a lack of science to value it or capital to fund it. The challenge is the structural failure of the Brazilian state to establish transparent and effective land governance. Without this governance, carbon markets operate in a regulatory vacuum that threatens community rights and the integrity of the ecosystems they intend to protect.

    1. Scientific and Economic Advances in Forest Valuation

    The panel demonstrated the existence of scientific tools and economic paradigms capable of valuing and financing forest conservation in a robust and precise manner.

    1.1. A Precise Methodology for Carbon Quantification

    Prof. Iraê (UNESP) presented a carbon quantification methodology developed in partnership with the University of Sassari (Italy) since 2015, which corrects significant flaws in IPCC standards.

    • Critique of IPCC Standard:
      • Soil Depth: The IPCC recommends sampling at 30 cm, but a study in the Atlantic Forest (Botucatu) showed that this depth ignores over 50% of carbon, located in the 30–100 cm layer.
      • Analysis Method: The standard method (Walkley-Black) underestimates carbon. Using an Elemental Analyzer (CHN) resulted in a 40% increase in measurement (from 129 to 181 ton/ha).
      • Litter (Serrapilheira): The IPCC's 50% conversion factor proved to be a 24% overestimation compared to direct CHN analysis (which revealed a content of 38%).
    • Results of the New Methodology:
      • Validation: The research was validated and published in the journal Scientific Reports from the Nature group.
      • Application in the Amazon: In a project in Acará (PA), in partnership with the Instituto Piabiru, the UNESP/CHN methodology recorded 211 ton/ha, a 90% increase over the 111 ton/ha that would be calculated by the IPCC method.

    1.2. The Sociobiodiversity Bioeconomy Paradigm

    Prof. Danilo Fernandes (UFPA/NAEA) contrasted two development models for the Amazon, defending a paradigm that recognizes the value of diversity and local knowledge.

    • Dichotomy of Paradigms:
      • Mechanical-Chemical Paradigm: Industrialist logic that treats nature as "dead," based on standardization and homogenization for scale gains (e.g., monocultures).
      • Organic Paradigm (Sociobiodiversity Bioeconomy): Logic that treats nature as "living," based on the management of diversity and traditional and endogenous knowledge.
    • Value and Threats:
      • Sociobiodiversity GDP: Using input-output matrices developed by Prof. Francisco de Assis Costa, the GDP of this "under-noted" economy in Pará was estimated at R$ 7 billion, with the export market (e.g., açaí) representing only 3%.
      • Threat of "Açaization": The speaker warned of the risk of açaí, a product of the organic paradigm, being captured by industrial logic. International market pressure for scale is forcing the "homogenization of floodplain areas," turning the product into a commodity and causing forest impoverishment.
      • Economic Dynamics: 20 years of data show that diversity-based systems grow (4.2%) but lose relative space to homogeneous grain systems, which grow faster (9.7%).

    1.3. The Interdependence between Agriculture and Forest

    Prof. Carlos Eduardo Serri (ESALQ/USP) argued that forest maintenance is an essential condition for the sustainability of agricultural production.

    • The Correct Metric: The "Carbon Footprint" metric is inadequate because it focus only on emissions. The correct metric is the "Carbon Balance" (inputs — outputs), which includes carbon sequestration in soil and vegetation.
    • Core Thesis: The speaker stated: "There is no possibility of this balance being neutral or negative if we do not keep the forest standing." Emissions from deforestation make the balance equation impossible to zero out.
    • Tools: The e-book "Soil Health in Tropical Ecosystems" was presented—a free work with over 150 authors—along with the "Soil Health Assessment Kit," tools to promote the resilience of production systems.

    1.4. Market Application and Generation of Social Benefits

    Prof. Alessandro Zabotto (Zabotto Ambiental) demonstrated, through case studies, how climate financing can fill gaps left by the State.

    • Case 1 (REDD+): The "Red Mais Aueté Project," on 345,000 hectares of the Aueté Indigenous Territory (PA), uses the VERRA methodology to generate carbon credits, with financing from Dutch investors.
    • Case 2 (PSA): The "Aripuanã-Guariba" project, on 145,000 hectares in southern Amazonas, uses the UNESP methodology to generate "conservation credits." In a region dominated by illegality and extreme social vulnerability, the project is financing:
      • Construction of masonry bathrooms with biodigester tanks.
      • Drilling of artesian wells.
      • Psychosocial support, which enabled the identification of children with autism and the guarantee of their social security rights.
    • Conclusion: The standing forest, through well-structured projects, can "transform lives," funding basic infrastructure and social rights.

    2. Structural Governance Failures and Market Threats

    The counterpoint to the opportunities was the denouncement of a flawed governance system that allows carbon markets to be instrumentalized for the expropriation of territories and the perpetuation of conflicts.

    2.1. Land Weakness as a Root Cause

    Prof. Richard Toziano (FAO/UN) identified the structural cause of all conflicts: Brazil's "profound land weakness."

    • Diagnosis: The State does not govern its territory because it does not know it. The existence of 50 million hectares of unallocated federal public lands in the Amazon creates a vacuum that is the main vector for land grabbing and occupation by "invisible" communities.

    2.2. The "Agrarian Metaverse" and the Instrumentalization of CAR

    Prof. Carlos Augusto Ramos (UFPA) conceptualized the mechanism by which land appropriation is legitimized in the digital world.

    • Concept: The "Agrarian Metaverse" is the "simulation of reality from the virtual world." The Rural Environmental Registry (CAR), being self-declaratory and implemented in a scenario of land chaos and low digital inclusion (less than 3% in 2012), has become the main tool for this simulation.

    2.3. Socio-Environmental Conflicts and "Carbon Neocolonialism"

    The combination of land weakness with the "agrarian metaverse" results in serious conflicts, transforming the carbon market into a threat.

    • Case 1 (Breves, PA): The company Eco Mapuá used a 90,000-hectare CAR overlapping two federal Extractive Reserves (RESEX) to "sell carbon credits" for families who did not even know the project existed.
    • Case 2 (Portel, PA): The municipality has 154% of its territory covered by CARs. Around 1 million hectares of carbon projects, valued at US$ 115 million, overlap settlements, with 30-year contracts signed without consultation and with "paltry" benefit-sharing (stoves, basic food baskets).
    • Conceptualization: This phenomenon was defined as "Carbon Neocolonialism" (Ramos) and "Green Grabbing" or "Green Neocolonization" (Toziano), where capital appropriates land for purposes of green speculation, expropriating the rights of traditional communities.

    2.4. Problematic Legal and Financial Structures

    Public-private structures and laws were criticized for potentially worsening the problem in the absence of governance.

    • Pará/LEAF Agreement: The Pará government's agreement to sell carbon credits to the LEAF Coalition (formed by governments and corporations like Amazon, Bayer, and Nestlé) was criticized for:
      • Price: The value of US 15 per ton, defined by the buyer LEAF, is derisory compared to the Social Cost of Carbon (SCC), which recent studies (Rennert et al., 2022, Nature) estimate at US 185 per ton.
      • Structure: The State's executive arm is a joint-stock company (CAAP S.A.), and the fund manager (Emergent) is based in Delaware, a tax haven, raising questions about transparency and sovereignty.
    • Fiagro Law: Law No. 14,130/2021, which establishes Agroindustrial Investment Funds, was pointed out as a possible loophole to Law No. 5,709/1971 (which restricts land purchases by foreigners), allowing international funds to bypass sovereignty control for land speculation.

    3. Paths toward the Solution and the Role of the University

    Despite the critical diagnosis, the panel also pointed to structural solutions and a new role for academia in building alternatives.

    3.1. Proposals for Effective Territorial Governance

    Prof. Richard Toziano presented a tripod of technical solutions to overcome land weakness:

    • Principles: Adoption of the "Voluntary Guidelines on the Responsible Governance of Tenure" (FAO, 2012) as a framework for rights protection.
    • Technical Standard: Implementation of the LADM (Land Administration Domain Model), ISO 19152, an international standard for a multi-purpose land registry focusing on rights, restrictions, and responsibilities.
    • Tool: Use of territorial intelligence platforms like "Terra Analytics" to integrate databases (CAR, SIGEF) and perform territorial due diligence.

    3.2. The Implementation Challenge and Misaligned Financing

    The final debate revealed that, even with solutions available, implementation is obstructed by misaligned public policies.

    • The Missing Link: The distribution of bioeconomy production, for example, is a basic logistics bottleneck preventing the advancement of the "organic paradigm."
    • Contradictory Financing: The most striking data was presented by Prof. Ramos: 92% of PRONAF (National Program for Strengthening Family Farming) resources in the Amazon are allocated to cattle ranching, the main driver of deforestation. This demonstrates that public financing works against bioeconomy and conservation.

    3.3. The University in the Territory: A New Model of Action

    The panel's conclusion pointed to the need for the university to overcome its traditional role and act directly in territories.

    • The Call to Action: Prof. Toziano questioned why universities are not submitting more projects to funds like the Amazon Fund, leaving this space to be occupied by NGOs and international funds.
    • The Practical Model: The "Casa da Floresta" (Forest House) initiative, a partnership between UNESP, Instituto Piabiru, and UFPA in the municipality of Acará (PA), was presented as the answer to this challenge. The project represents the university physically installed in the territory, listening to the community's demands and directly applying its research in concrete solutions, such as low-carbon housing projects using local materials.

    FINAL CONSIDERATIONS

    The panel "Challenges for Maintaining the Standing Forest" proved to be an important synthesis space between cutting-edge scientific research, critical economic analysis, and practical community experiences. The paradigmatic tensions between the industrial-homogenizing model and the ecological-diversified model emerged as a central issue, with direct implications for the viability of forest conservation.

    Convergence around three points was notable:

    1. Need for Precision: Accurate quantifications of carbon, economic values, and environmental impacts are fundamental for policy legitimacy;
    2. Necessary Integration: Forest preservation, sustainable production, and community well-being are not contradictory, but complementary when managed appropriately;
    3. University Engagement: A proactive role for academia in research, innovation, transparency, and dialogue with public authorities is imperative.

    The event concludes by recognizing that "the word is partnership"—between institutions, between kinds of knowledge, between government and communities, between Brazil and the world—as a sine qua non requirement for success in keeping forests standing while producing wealth and guaranteeing human well-being in diverse tropical contexts.
