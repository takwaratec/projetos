    Vegetal Polyurethane

    High-strength polymer, substitute for human prostheses, volatile varnishes, waterproofers, foams, and binders, is produced from vegetable oils of renewable and biodegradable sources.

    Dr. Gilberto Chierice, 1943–2019, USC-USP — Photo: Lucio Bernardo Junior/Chamber of Deputies 2015

    Polyurethanes are polymers, smaller molecules that chemically combine to form long, branched molecules. All known plastics are examples of polymers: Polycarbonate (PC), Polyvinyl chloride (PVC), Polystyrene (PS), Polypropylene (PP), Polyethylene Terephthalate (PET), Plexiglass or acrylic (PMMA), and polyurethanes (PU).

    The popularization of these materials allowed the productive society to create countless forms of application for a multitude of items present almost everywhere on the planet, either as objects of use or as discarded waste. A study conducted by WWF in 2017 pointed out that seven countries together produce nearly 300 million tons of plastic waste per year. The leader was the USA with 70 million and 34% recycling, followed by China (54m-21%), India (19m-5%), Brazil (12m and a measly 1.28% recycled), Indonesia (9m-3%), Russia (8m-3%), Germany (8m-37%), United Kingdom (7m-31%), Japan (7m-5%), and Canada (6m-21%). Half of all plastics polluting the world were produced starting from the year 2000, and 75% of all plastic ever produced has been discarded. Some plastics can take 200 to 500 years to decompose in nature.

    Polyurethane comprises a chain of organic units joined by urethane links, organic compounds that share the same functional group (-NH(CO)O-). It is widely used in paints, waterproofers, rigid and flexible foams, high-performance adhesives, fiber composites, preservatives, carpets, elastomers, and rigid plastic parts, among many others.

    Its creation is attributed to the German chemist Otto Bayer at the beginning of World War II, as a replacement for rubber used in machines and weaponry. Its composition is obtained from a polyol, such as propylene oxide, propylene/ethylene oxides, tetramethylene oxide, and glycols (PPGs), combined with an organic catalyst based on isocyanates, which causes a polycondensation reaction. It is generally complemented with the addition of volatile elements, such as acetone, methylene chloride, and fluorocarbons—toxic substances derived from petroleum, pollutants, and carcinogens.

    Vegetal PU is also a creation of Otto Bayer in the 1940s (he who creates the poison always has an antidote); however, it was only commercially synthesized in 1994 at the University of São Carlos-SP, Brazil, by chemistry professor Gilberto Chierice. Meeting a demand from ELETROBRÁS, Prof. Chierice created the patent for the first vegetal elastomer in history, which was sold to the company and used in underground cabling with much higher durability and resistance than conventional ones produced with mineral PU. With the money raised, the professor invested everything in a laboratory at the university and dedicated himself to research with vegetal PU in the production of human bone prostheses, as a replacement for titanium and other metals used to this day.

    Prof. Chierice's research was triggered alongside that of Biodiesel, developed at the same university and which uses castor oil (Ricinus Communis L.) as a fundamental component. Castor polyol, combined with MDI—a class of more elaborate isocyanates that allow greater freedom in formulations—produces a high-strength polymer, preserving the bactericidal and fungicidal characteristics inherent to castor oil. Ricinus Communis L. has fatty acids very similar to human fats, creating a harmony that inhibits any rejection of the implanted prosthesis.

    The PUs developed by Prof. Chierice applied to biomedicine were produced by Poliquil, a company also created by this genius of innovation in the city of Araraquara-SP, becoming shared, for research purposes, with other scientists in the world. The technological innovation presented and proven with numerous tests in countries like Argentina and Chile was named in the USA by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) as RG Kryptonite, in a reference to Superman, the hero from planet Krypton who, in 1938, traveled to Earth in crystal particles to save our planet from the idiocies of the human race in Joe Shuster and Jerry Siegel's comics.

    Bionic bone prostheses, cranial reconstitution of poly-traumatized patients, and skin rejuvenation through micro-filaments implanted under sagging dermis—its use in Brazil is still incipient despite the importance of the discovery and hundreds of theses published worldwide. It was being applied in works like those of the Heart Institute (INCOR-USP), where a portable and extra-corporeal functional organ was created to improve the quality of life of patients waiting for an implant.

    The regulation and adoption of this material in the world market encounter barriers that even Superman couldn't tear down: the bureaucracy of regulatory bodies in Brazil, such as ANVISA, which takes on average three years to issue a Certificate of Good Manufacturing Practices, has not to this day granted the requested petition (IPEA 2006 data). In the USA, castor oil derivatives are strictly controlled due to Ricin, considered one of the most potent toxins of plant origin found to date: the protein of power, of Anthrax. Prof. Chierice used to say that castor oil is a valuable natural resource: "it's like plowing the land with a Rolls-Royce," he said. He considered its use in biodiesel production a waste, as less noble oils like soy or peanut could be used for that. He passed away at age 75 in 2019 and, as a retiree, also created the Cancer Pill.

    Castor oil derivatives are old acquaintances of humanity, used in the mummification technique of ancient Egypt and as fuel for the lamps of slaves in India. The oil is currently used in the composition of cosmetics, bactericidal and fungicidal ointments, nutritional supplements for plants and animals, greases, adhesives, and automotive lubricants. Brazil, which was once the largest producer of castor oil in the world, is today in third position with 6% of world production, dominated by China and India—the granary of production for "developed" countries that dictate what can or cannot be produced for consumption on the planet.

    There are still some small companies in Brazil, in the São Carlos region (western São Paulo State), created and inspired by the grit and enthusiasm of Prof. Chierice, investing in the production of vegetal PUs for civil construction. These are used as waterproofers in the most diverse applications: industrial floors, roofs, sewage treatment tanks (STPs), drinking water storage, adhesives, and high-density foams used in expansion joints, modeling blocks, and bonding of various types. Several applications have been researched and applied in the composition of fiber aggregates, in wood as a replacement for varnishes, adhesives for laminates, cardboard, packaging, etc., and it allows its use in any segment occupied by conventional mineral PUs. They are biodegradable in up to 30 years, non-toxic, and sustainable.

    For eight years, I have been conducting research on the use of this product, which is still only manufactured in Brazil, for the waterproofing and bonding of Bamboo. The product guarantees resistance to weathering, does not spread flames, and unlike similar products—varnishes and adhesives of mineral origin—it has no smell and does not require periodic maintenance every year. Its competitiveness in the market is aggravated by the lack of national inputs, such as MDI, which is imported because the only manufacturer in the world, Basf, closed its activities in the country.

    This article is an appeal to the inhabitants of this planet, especially Brazilians, who have the obligation to know, defend, and spread its use, stimulating its production and accessibility. With special thanks to Imperveg Polímeros Vegetais and Kehl Coat for the support in distributing samples for studies, pioneering spirit, courage, and resilience to the setbacks, vices, and the dismantling of national policy in recent decades. A simple and just tribute to Professor Dr. Gilberto Orivaldo Chierice (1943–2019), whose memory is gradually being erased by the eraser of ignorance.
