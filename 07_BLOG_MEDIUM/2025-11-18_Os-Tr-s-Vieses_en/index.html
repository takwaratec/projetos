    The Three Biases Shaping Brazil's Climate Contradiction at COP30

    1 - Brazil's Climate Leadership Under Scrutiny

    Originally published on Medium on 2025-11-18. Integrated into the structured technical archive (Advocacy 5.1).

    The Three Biases Shaping Brazil's Climate Contradiction at COP30

    1 - Brazil's Climate Leadership Under Scrutiny

    On the stage of COP30 in Belém, Brazil projects to the world the image of a reborn climate leader, ready to guide nations toward a new green economy anchored in the "standing forest." The ambition is undeniable and the platform, strategic. However, beneath the surface of diplomacy and official rhetoric, a glaring contradiction emerges between the leadership the country claims and the policies it practices internally. This dissonance is no accident, nor a mere management stumble.

    The blatant contradictions in the Brazilian climate agenda are, in fact, the direct result of a deep internal political dispute, where three competing biases vie for control of the narrative and national policy. These are the forces that shape Brazil's double face—a country that simultaneously defends the Amazon and plans to drill it. To understand Brazil's real trajectory, it is necessary to deconstruct these three biases:

    1. Nationalist Developmentism, which prioritizes extractivist sovereignty.
    2. Institutional Greenwashing, which masks contradictions with a selective narrative.
    3. The Exclusion of Climate Justice, which silences alternatives in favor of the market.

    Examining each of these biases reveals how Brazilian climate policy is shaped by antagonistic forces. Let's start with the most powerful and deeply rooted one.

    2 - Nationalist Developmentism and the Fossil Paradox

    The first and most potent bias defining Brazilian policy is "nationalist developmentism." With deep historical roots in the Brazilian state, this ideological current views the environmental agenda as a potential threat to national sovereignty. Its logic is clear: the exploitation of natural resources, such as oil, is not just an economic activity, but an assertion of national power and autonomy. Any opposing pressure is viewed with suspicion, as an attempt at external interference.

    This view manifests in the most glaring contradiction of COP30: the decision to actively promote the expansion of the fossil fuel frontier at the Mouth of the Amazon. While the world gathers in Belém to discuss the end of the fossil era, the Brazilian government moves forward with plans that the Instituto Socioambiental (ISA) classifies as a "shadow of non-sense" and that the 2025 GOGEL report, from NGOs Urgewald and Instituto Arayara, denounces as "alarming hypocrisy."

    The numbers illustrate the dimension of the paradox:

    • Petrobras, a mostly state-owned company, is the 15th largest exploration company in the world.
    • The exploration of just one basin at the Mouth of the Amazon (FZA-M-59) has the potential to release 4.7 billion tons of CO2 equivalent—more than the emissions of the entire Brazilian agricultural sector for six years.

    This policy does not arise in a vacuum. It is the expression of a robust political faction, articulated by figures like Aldo Rebelo, who considers environmental pressure a form of "internationalization of the Amazon" and NGOs a "parallel government." In this doctrine, defending oil drilling on the Amazon coast is, paradoxically, an act of defense of sovereignty. To manage this fundamental tension, the federal government resorts to a carefully crafted communication strategy.

    3 - Institutional Greenwashing and Narrative Manipulation

    To mediate the inevitable collision between nationalist developmentism and the pressures of the international climate community, the Brazilian government adopts the bias of "institutional greenwashing." This is a strategy to build a narrative of climate success, diverting attention from policies that directly contradict that same narrative.

    The main tool of this strategy is the selective use of deforestation data. The government presents its "diplomatic trump card" to the world: data from the PRODES system, the annual "MRI" that offers the definitive rate of clear-cutting and shows a "significant drop." However, it deliberately ignores the inconvenient alerts from the DETER system, which operates in real-time and reveals an 8.4% increase in deforestation alerts in the same period, driven by an alarming 245.7% increase in forest fires. The choice is political: celebrate the consolidated data of the past and minimize the emerging crisis of the present.

    The official rhetoric follows the same pattern. President Lula publicly admits the country's "difficulties and contradictions," but reframes them as a necessary balance. The justification that oil profits will be used to fund the energy transition is the pinnacle of this logic. It is a classic climate fallacy, requiring the expansion of fossil activity in the short term to, hypothetically, fund its reduction in the future, ignoring the urgency imposed by the global carbon budget.

    This strategy of concealment is evident in the analysis of Brazil's Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC). According to the Observatório do Clima, Brazilian targets are insufficient, allow for the continuity of legal deforestation, and, crucially, maintain an eloquent "fossil silence" regarding expansion plans. This narrative of convenience only holds because a third bias operates to actively silence the voices and solutions that challenge it.

    4 - The Exclusion of Climate Justice in Favor of the Market

    The greenwashing strategy is only possible because a third bias is actively operating: the exclusion of climate justice issues in favor of technocratic and market solutions. In practice, there are two COPs happening in Belém, each with an opposing ideological agenda. One takes place in the air-conditioned corridors of the "Blue Zone," the official UN space; the other, on the streets, under the Amazon sun.

    Inside the Blue Zone, the agenda is dominated by finance and markets. Discussions focus on the integration of carbon markets (Article 6) and the structuring of trillion-dollar financing packages. It is in this space that real power manifests: a record number of more than 1,600 fossil fuel industry lobbyists were accredited for the event. This means that 1 in every 25 participants represented fossil interests, and that this delegation was larger than the official delegations of all countries, except for the host, Brazil.

    In direct contrast, the "People's Summit" and the March for Climate, which brought together 70,000 people, present a radically different agenda based on rights and prohibitions. Their central demands are not for carbon credits, but for direct action: the immediate end of oil exploration and the demarcation of indigenous lands as the most effective climate solution.

    The Brazilian government adopts a cynical posture toward this dualism. It uses the existence of the protests as a symbol of the country's democratic vitality, but systematically ignores the essential demands raised by them. Brazil uses the image of Amazonian peoples to legitimize its global leadership but excludes their proposals from the negotiating table, favoring a "green industrialization" negotiated with the same polluters the conference should be fighting.

    Conclusion: Beyond Hypocrisy, the Political Dispute

    Brazil's climate policy at COP30 is defined by a web of contradictions. Behind it, however, there is no incompetence, but a deliberate political choice, shaped by three clear biases: the nationalist developmentism that drives fossil expansion, the institutional greenwashing that conceals it, and the exclusion of climate justice that marginalizes real and rights-based alternatives.

    Brazil's double face is not, therefore, a sign of weakness, but a reflection of a deep and unreconciled internal political dispute. The contradictions are the logical result of a government trying to reconcile the irreconcilable: pleasing a historical extraction-based base while projecting an image of environmental leadership to the world. COP30, hosted in the Amazon, exposes this tension as unsustainable. The defining question for Brazil is not whether it can project leadership, but whether that leadership will be built on the solid foundation of coherence and justice, or on the fragile pillars of contradiction and exclusion.
