### Bamboo and Carbon

#### The plant that sequesters the most is the one that emits the most CO2

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*BgGdevboSVVReY8dj5VXLg.jpeg)

The current planetary scenario, considering only population growth and forest destruction, makes scientists believe, based on [stochastic studies](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-020-63657-6), that humanity has a 10% chance of surviving the catastrophic effects of climate change predicted for the coming years, potentially marking the end of the human species on Earth in less than five decades.

> Experts [also claim](https://brasil.mongabay.com/2018/07/as-reais-emissoes-carbono-do-brasil-associadas-florestas-podem-passar-despercebidas-pelo-acordo-paris/) that there is under-reporting in the emissions reports of several countries, as they disregard fires and spontaneous forest degradation as potential emission agents.

Bamboo is often cited as the plant species that sequesters the most CO2 from the atmosphere due to its fast production cycle, which starts from 2 years of age and repeats annually for up to more than 100 years. This means that a bamboo forest can sequester from 50t to 400t of CO2 per hectare annually (depending on the species), while eucalyptus, after 7 years, sequesters around 40t to 60t per hectare only once.

What few say is that to fix this sequestration so it can be accounted for, it is necessary for the bamboo to be harvested annually and for all the collected biomass to be utilized. We know there are numerous bamboo forests scattered around the world; the largest is native and much of it is in the state of Acre, Amazonas, and mostly in Peru, forming a patch of approximately 200,000 km² in length.

Several articles point to bamboo as a natural competitor to the arboreal forest, with extreme invasive potential, quickly occupying areas deforested by human action. Added to this, numerous forests of native and exotic species, some invasive, brought by colonizers and oriental immigrants, occupy an extensive territory in the country, especially in the Atlantic Forest.

> In the opinion of some bamboo experts, it is impossible to enter the forests of Acre for commercial extraction; the same applies to other scattered forests, many of them in areas of high population density and marked human development deficit.

This means that there may be almost 10 million hectares of bamboo forests in Brazilian territory without any use. Furthermore, based on the decomposition time of bamboo, which at each annual production cycle can vary from seven to thirty years, and considering that in its flowering the entire bamboo grove dies to produce seeds, it is likely that through spontaneous decomposition of biomass in unmanaged forests, the amount of CO2 emissions may be even greater than what is sequestered every year.

> Brazil accounts for almost 20% of the more than 1,300 bamboo species known in the world; although many of them have been extinct by human action, it is estimated that about 130 of them are endemic, that is, they only occur in Brazil.

**What to do then? Let the arboreal forests suffocate with the invasion and emission of carbon not fixed by bamboo?**

The bamboo production chain in Brazil has been gaining space in public power since the enactment of the [Bamboo Law](http://www.planalto.gov.br/ccivil_03/_ato2011-2014/2011/lei/l12484.htm) in September 2011, which instituted the National Policy to Encourage Sustainable Management and Cultivation of Bamboo (PNMCB) in recognition of this important productive segment that sustains itself on a tradition still inexpressive in popular culture.

From 2017, when Brazil became part of an important international technical cooperation network composed of more than thirty countries, [INBAR](https://www.inbar.int/), the Ministry of Science, Technology, Innovation, and Communications (MCTIC), which had already been supporting this movement, began to promote several actions such as seminars, congresses, and cooperation agreements intended to define the public policies to be adopted for the development of this important production chain in the country.

The dialogue between society and public power has been conducted through representatives of important education and research institutions, as well as some segments organized through associations of producers, industries, and professionals in the field. The discussions point to a promising future for the country due to this natural collection that inspired the Law in its article 3, item III:

> "The guidelines of the PNMCB are the development of poles of sustainable management, cultivation, and processing of bamboo, especially in the regions of greatest occurrence of natural stocks of the plant, in regions whose agricultural production is based on family production units and around centers generating technologies applicable to the product."

A recent panorama of national and global scenarios was published by the Center for Strategic Management and Studies (CGEE) in the analytical [report](https://www.cgee.org.br/documents/10195/734063/3368_Nota+T%C3%A9cnica+Cadeia+Produtiva+do+Bambu.pdf) of the Symposium "Bamboo Economy in Brazil: Technology and Innovation in the Productive Chain — Perspectives and Challenges," held in 2018 at the Federation of Industries of the State of São Paulo (FIESP).

What can be stated by the optimistic discourse and the systemic approach evidenced at the opening of the event is that there is a very great will to make this Productive Chain function; however, the biggest challenge still consists in making this intention public, making bamboo a national passion. Notwithstanding the qualification of the actors who act on behalf of society, this representation is still incipient for national decision-making regarding the inclusion of all segments addressed and those omitted within a Governmental Agenda.

Considering the urgency of fulfilling important agendas already in progress, such as [Agenda 2030](http://www.agenda2030.org.br/sobre/), the [Pluriannual Plan 2020–2023](https://www.gov.br/economia/pt-br/assuntos/planejamento-e-orcamento/plano-plurianual-ppa), the [Paris Agreement](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acordo_de_Paris_%282015%29), and the reduction of fires and deforestation, added at this time to the economic and social damage caused by COVID-19, it is necessary that efforts be directed towards immediate implementation emergency actions, as Bamboo is an important element for reaching the goals proposed in these agreements, especially in the eradication of poverty and the reduction of CO2 emissions.

> For this, the management of existing bamboo forests, which are in decomposition, without use and carbon fixation, must be considered in the first instance.

Federal-level government actions need to value the popularization of the economic and socio-environmental potential of bamboo through decrees, ordinances, tax incentives, standardizations, and awareness campaigns. Insert bamboo into the public machine across the national sphere, in the armed forces, in ministries, state secretariats; as a mandatory field or transverse theme in agricultural, biological, human, and technological sciences, in vocational training schools, in the "S System," technical schools, and federal institutes; train EMATER technicians to support small producers, identify and establish biomass collection and processing centers.

Brazil, despite the great availability of bamboo forest resources, advanced scientific qualification, and high potential for industrial development, still lacks specialized labor to perform the countless functions that encompass the use of bamboo in its production chains.

Fortunately, there are research centers scattered across the country in various universities, and the expressive private initiative that has been consolidating in recent years allows an integrated action to achieve impressive results in the short term, considering the inclusion of the immense underserved population, the unemployed, and segments affected by the pandemic as the main axis of the solution for the use of this true wasted arsenal.

> More transparency and inclusion are needed in decision-making, public calls, and allocation of scarce resources so that public policies applied to bamboo eventually comprehensively address the most serious problems of society. With ten years since its enactment, it was expected that many of these actions would already be implemented, as determined by Law.

A good path being followed, albeit still obtusely, is what the development of this production chain in China shows us. Recognized as the technological center of bamboo for many centuries, starting from the 1980s with the Cultural Revolution and opening to the West, China began to invest in strengthening bamboo-populated provinces by installing technological training and research centers, which extended to all development areas in the country.

To give an idea of the socio-environmental gain achieved with these measures, the growth of bamboo artifact production allowed the increase of plantations, which were approximately 1 million hectares—counting all native species and totaling 60% of all known species—to 6 million hectares planted in 2013, constituting the largest artificial bamboo forest in the world with 4.5 million hectares of a single species, becoming today the country that most imports bamboo on the planet because it has nowhere else to plant.

As a result, China, which had come to occupy first place in the ranking of countries with more carbon emissions, moved in 2019 to eleventh position behind Monaco and Peru, and the capital Beijing, which was third in the ranking, ceased to figure among the 200 most polluted cities in the world.

> Much is due to the energy development with the reduction of coal burning; however, the immense managed bamboo forest that joins 19 productive provinces plays an important role in these results.

Chinese experts claim that only 60% of the biomass generated with bamboo in the country is fixed, and this counting on massive investments and a surprising rate of utilization. The goal is to increasingly raise these results; the Chinese government has publicly assumed the commitment to zero emissions by 2060.

Some Latin American countries like Colombia, Chile, and Ecuador preserve a millennial culture of bamboo use and sustain advanced training and research centers, with bamboo being standardized for civil construction and social interest housing programs, in addition to commercial production chains.

> Examples and partners are abundant; INBAR, with all its expertise in advising governments, depends on a political guideline to better perform its support function.

Brazil is already advancing with a standard for construction with bamboo, and some states like Santa Catarina and Goiás have mobilized for the creation of legal devices; notably, only Acre has already [anticipated](https://www.normasbrasil.com.br/norma/portaria-5-2017-ac_343785.html) in legalizing the exploitation of native species in protection and conservation areas, being the Brazilian state with the most experience in forest management.

However, experts say they don't quite know what to do with the immense biomass that some already identified areas can offer.
Despite all the possibilities of use and all the industrial segments identified, strategies for the implementation of management, storage, and processing of existing bamboo forests in the country have not yet been thought of.

Instead, under the pretext of recovering degraded areas, and even as a branch of agribusiness, it is preferred to spread the planting of more exotic species, even reaching a prospect of artificial cultivation of 20 million hectares, which is pharaonic, given that China remained for millennia with only 1 million but only in the last 40 years increased its canopy to 6 million, absorbing almost the equivalent of our population in its production chain.

In this line of reasoning, we will have to mobilize the country to produce Chinese bamboo for China or else transform ourselves into the first Nation of Bamboo-growers in the world to handle all this planting.

May God bless any path, or we will disappear from the map and only the bamboo will be left to tell the story.
