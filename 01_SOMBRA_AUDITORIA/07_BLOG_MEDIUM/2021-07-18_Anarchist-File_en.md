### Anarchist File

### July 18, 2021 Today is my birthday!

*“Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent.” Ludwig Wittgenstein*

I am 55 years old and I woke up wanting to write. I wanted to speak of how the days were and are beautiful, of how life is beautiful, and I well could do so if I were to speak only of myself.
No life is a bed of roses; those who know me know well how many ups and downs I've been through and will yet dare to go through. I could boast of some privileges, like being born into a family of good character, being taught to read by my mother before entering school, and enjoying a childhood full of books and fun. Vacations at my grandparents' farm in Minas, PlayCenter, zoo, club, restaurant, and beach. I woke up wanting to write what memory brings me, so I never forget where I came from and where I will go, in case "parks" one day snatches me at the threshold of the journey and I can know what winds guide me.

I was born in São Paulo and matured precociously during the most inglorious years of this country, with the exception of now. Those were dark times; back then, news didn't run free as it does today, and it was forbidden to speak of repression at home, in the streets, and at schools. Our house was a kind of consulate for people from Minas; my parents left Minas in 1959 and little by little offered support to relatives and friends, providing shelter until each one achieved the long-dreamed job in the "big city."

I remember, as if it were yesterday, the arrival of a cousin of my father who was an artist, kind of effeminate as they said, who had come to audition for actor in a theater downtown. I was a child of five or six, but he talked to me like an adult. One day, all smiles, he dressed up nicely and left for the audition skipping. The last image I kept of him was his arrival home in the middle of the night, all tattered and his face bloody.

My mother was an admirable woman; she was educated in the Sisters' school and faced all the prejudices of the time with her head held high. She wore lipstick, red dresses, Lee jeans, and was the first (female) salesperson in the street sales team of Baú da Felicidade, so it is told here in the city. She told me it was necessary to eat many lambari fish to learn to swim as well and cross the Rio Verde as she did. Later, in São Paulo, she specialized in creating wedding invitations and business cards—an art director, besides being a commercial representative for several classical literature publishers and encyclopedias. She liked the Beatles, Secos e Molhados, Roberto Carlos, and Caetano Veloso. She drank cachaça at bar tables and frequented Candomblé houses.

With the arrival of children—me in '66, another in '67, another in '69, until completing the "volleyball team" with three pairs in 1976—the discussions at home were about how a woman could raise children and continue working outside the home, and she even considered a motorcycle as a solution to the problem. In 1977, taken by alcoholism, which kept her distant from machismo and oppression, my mother left, victim of a forced withdrawal that led her to the only substance that would have passed the scrutiny of vigilance in those times of crisis, when not even aerosol deodorant was allowed at home, and she drank the muriatic acid used to clean the septic tank.

Back then, I already scratched out some English and liked talking to older people. I studied in public school and liked participating in the band, Olympic gymnastics, theater, and visiting the library. With my mother's passing, my father, who was an insurance surveyor, sometimes took me to work. We lived in Guarulhos, and he would drive to the Santana Metro for the S. Bento station and take some laps around the center. Sometimes he would ask me to deliver correspondence to some company office, and I would take the opportunity to wander around. I loved maps and street guides; I walked alone through Praça da República, Sé, São Bento, Consolação, Sta Efigênia. I knew the names of the streets, knew the tourist spots: Municipal Theater, Luz Station, Pinacoteca, Mappim! I would go back to SAGA at Barão de Itapetininga 175, all proud of not having needed to ask anyone for information nor spend my phone tokens. I remember his phone number to this day, which I never needed to dial: 35 21 81.

Some time later, my father was fired, and in a cascade effect, it seems the whole family fell. The country was going through a recession like no other, so history tells; I didn't understand any of that, but I perceived the disastrous effects on the family. At school, it was no different; many of the teachers, who were military men, began to abuse austerity and imposed barracks routines: formation, flag-raising, marching band, school Olympics, and gate searches. It didn't take long, and I was expelled for subversive behavior. My father had already gone to Brasília with Serena, the '78/'79 Caravan, to take a post at Sul América, and when things improved, he would come back to get us.

By that time, we had a bar there in Jd. São Paulo, and when he heard the fact, from there he recalled a saying that was much spoken: "he who doesn't study, works!" I could no longer enroll by order of the generals, so at 13, I started working at Bar e Mercearia Irmãos Resck. But in that time, those who held the fort were Me and Uncle Bueno. On Wednesdays, it was necessary to wake up at dawn and take two or three buses to serve the market vendors' breakfast before daybreak, and the shift was almost always until nightfall, never before washing the floors, counters, display cases, and bathrooms. When I learned to go alone, Bueno also got a job, and the food shelves had long given way to pinball, pool, and dominoes. I took care of everything until Uncle Resck arrived from work to clear the tables, lower the shutters, and roll the card games.

It was located near Carandiru, which I remember from trips in Bernardão, the '51 Chevrolet, or in that nameless Aerowillis that lost its brakes on the old road to Santos. I would look at those walls passing by and imagine a hidden Castle, like in the stories of Don Quixote and Ivanhoe. In my first "job," the castles crumbled like sand, but the real world shouldn't seem strange, inhuman, or give me reason for despair, because Jesus had already suffered for all of us on the cross. So said the late Uncles Resck and Bueno, ministers of the holy Catholic church, who "inherited" our house, which I saw being built with the sweat of my parents and is even today under usucaption litigation. It was April 1982 when my father came to get us with Serena, and with Grandma Dirce in command, we all went to the capital.

On the way, my father tried to convince me to study at the military college, that everything was already arranged, and I, looking at that sea of red earth passing by the window, only remembered the advice of the group of high school friends and the hippies from Praça da República: "go there and throw a bomb at that congress!" In the second month of public school, I was gently invited to leave after an essay titled, suggested by the teacher, "What would you do if you were the President of the Republic?", which ended up via fax in my father's office. Already knowing the saying, I took a test at Bradesco for office boy and was admitted as a clerk at the UN service post, where only I and the head of the post, who was the cashier, took care of the whole mess. From there, I requested a transfer to São Paulo and ended up at the Gasômetro agency, where I watched over the super HD of the first online agency in the country and contributed to the union in strike mobilization as a telex operator.

The following year, in '83, I resigned and returned to Brasília. I managed to enroll in the music school and had an internship as a teaching assistant in an art school, CRESÇA, where I got involved in political articulation movements. I learned the craft of ceramics, practiced yoga, and even became a live model for anatomical drawing classes. I participated in innovative projects like a construction cooperative with earth, "Mãos ao Barro," and even followed the construction of a ferrocement dome in 1985 in Brazlândia. I integrated into the alternative movement that preceded the creation of UNIPAZ, when it was still the UniverCidade project, which operated in the dressing rooms of the Martins Pena room of the National Theater. There I learned about astrology, tarot, homeopathy, iridology, permaculture, acupuncture, biodanza, and all the trends, theories, and teachings that inspired the "New Age."

I returned to São Paulo and graduated as a final artist at Senac. Where there were people willing, there was always a revolution; we created an institution to support disabled people, while I paid for the course by making invitations and cards in the same print shop of my paternal uncle where my mother had worked. I had the privilege and honor of knowing and working under the baton of Paulo Freire and Father Ticão in a community project in Hermelino Matarazzo. Back in Brasília in '87, I became an environmentalist and as a photographer—a trade inherited from the surveyor role that was always present in the family—I began documenting catastrophes and wonders of nature, including human nature. I collected rolls of horrors committed by a cattle rancher for years, who later became a minister in 2004 and ended up as the president of the senate, presiding over the last impeachment that ended the dream of revolution.

I discovered Macintoshes in São Paulo in '86 and later digital photography in '96. I gave courses at the presidency, newspapers, and agencies. I opened a publishing house with award-winning books, kitsch magazines, and the first weekly cultural guide of Brasília, Caleidoscópio, in '95. I designed the university magazine and resigned when censorship, imposed by a Jabuti Award-winning editor, ordered the cutting of an environmentalist article that hurt the honor of his boss, Renan. I ended my career in the basements of academia, editing the "Bases for the discussion of the Brazilian Agenda 21" in 2002.

Stacking up denunciations and disillusions, I never needed to present a diploma, except in 2005 when I passed a university entrance exam and couldn't assume the position for not being able to prove I had finished high school. I continue to work as if nothing has changed, and in fact, nothing has changed; in truth, it has only gotten worse. I remember having staged the opening of Rio92 when the term ecology was still an unknown; we defended the preservation of the remaining 50% of the Cerrado, which by a miracle of statistics still remains standing, even after decades of mismanagement, fires, and destruction. Vital Farias is still a success with his "Saga da Amazônia"; indigenous people and the landless continue to flee, homeless but with feet on the ground, while the "experts" teach treating bamboo with cockroach killer and consider iron and cement a "Bio-construction" technique.

In the last ten years dealing with bamboo, my ideas still cause discomfort in discussion groups. As if a being from another planet arrived and out of nowhere sentenced that the local culture is totally improper for the standards of its species. As a volunteer, I faced a board of doctors and spokespeople of power with their retrograde ideas, thirsty for fame and domination. I approved a project for 7.5 million and they reject my theory for lack of references, while they burn their eyelashes for an innovation prize.

Without a diploma, without fame, and without money, I choose this July 18th to write these memories, which in the future will remind me that I am alive through stubbornness, for believing in the dream of the impossible construction. The theory of p and not-p teaches that every proposition can be true, even if the reality of the facts is false, and the reverse is also true. Knowing how to use language doesn't mean that what is well said is The Truth; written or spoken words only have absolute meaning when they express the experience lived by the author, and only he can make the judgment of value.
Yes, I am alive, full, and overflowing with revolution projects!

And this was the labor of this day.

Fabio “Takwara” Resck
