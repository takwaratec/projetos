### The Silent Scandal of Brazilian Bamboo: When Sustainability Hides Carcinogenic Toxicity

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*CnoUoSekd-ivHajGiakB6A.png)

Toxic sustainability from barefoot workers.

#### 93% of the environmental impact of artisanal bamboo production is associated with human carcinogenic toxicity. And almost no one is talking about it.

I have dedicated more than a decade of my life to bamboo. I studied its qualities and characteristics, tested non-toxic treatment alternatives, visited producing communities, and engaged with national and international researchers. Throughout all these years, I have sounded the alarm—in lectures, articles, and public workshop, voluntarily—about the risks of the indiscriminate use of toxic chemical compounds in the treatment of this extraordinary plant. I was repeatedly ignored. Worse: I was ridiculed for questioning practices **"consecrated"** by masters with decades of experience.

Today, in October 2025, on the eve of COP30 in Belém—an event that will position Brazil as a global leader in the Amazonian bioeconomy—we finally have scientific evidence that can no longer be ignored, minimized, or ridiculed. And it is staggering.

A study published in April 2025 in the *Brazilian Journal of Science*, conducted by researchers from the São Paulo State University (UNESP) and the Federal Institute of São Paulo (IFSP), performed a Life Cycle Assessment of the artisanal production of *Guadua angustifolia* bamboo poles in Rio Branco, Acre. The result is a verdict: 93% of the total environmental impact is associated with Human Carcinogenic Toxicity, arising from the use of boric acid and borax in chemical treatment.

We are not talking about a theoretical risk. We are talking about workers exposed daily to substances that can cause cancer. We are talking about toxic effluents being dumped directly into the Amazonian soil, with 10% of the water used in treatment contaminating springs and rivers that supply indigenous and riverside communities. We are talking about an industry that presents itself as "sustainable" while perpetuating practices that would put any other sector in the crosshairs of rigorous environmental audits.

And most painful of all: this contamination is not restricted to Acre. It has been spread nationally by a network of educational institutions, bioconstruction schools, and permaculture centers that, well-intentioned but ill-informed (or deliberately negligent), have taught these techniques to thousands of people over three decades.

### The Spreading Network: From North to South, Toxicity Traveled

Mentally map Brazil. Now locate:

* EBIOBAMBU (Visconde de Mauá/RJ): first national school for construction with bamboo, founded in 2002.
* CERBAMBU (Ravena/MG): reference center that has trained more than 10,000 students since 1985.
* TIBÁ Institute (Visconde de Mauá/RJ): bioarchitecture courses with bamboo treatment modules.
* Ameríndia Atelier (Poços de Caldas/MG): regular courses on bamboo "immunization."
* Sítio Pau d'Água (Piracaia/SP): 23 classes already trained.
* Eco Sítio Bambusa (SC): center for testing and disseminating techniques.
* Goiás Bamboo Network: founded in 2013 at the Federal University of Goiás.
* Bioarquitetura (Brasília-DF): More than 20,000m² built.
* Dozens of other centers, ecovillages, and permaculture institutes.

All, or almost all, teaching the same recipe: dipping bamboo in a solution of boric acid and borax for 5 to 15 days. Some also teach the use of CCA (Chromated Copper Arsenate)—a preservative containing arsenic, classified as a proven human carcinogen (Group I) by the International Agency for Research on Cancer.

The expansion reached the digital environment: on the Hotmart platform, online courses teach "all the steps to **TREAT** bamboo," potentially reaching thousands of people without any supervision regarding safety protocols.

Conservative estimate: between 15,000 and 20,000 people trained in bamboo techniques in the last three decades in Brazil. The vast majority learned methods that science now proves are toxic to workers and the environment.

And where do these people apply this knowledge? In ecovillages located in Permanent Preservation Areas (APPs), in buffer zones of Conservation Units, in tourist spots in highlands and plateaus, in rural properties near springs and rivers.

Brazil has between 300 to 400 ecovillages and intentional communities, many of them concentrated in high-biodiversity regions—Serra da Mantiqueira, Chapada dos Veadeiros, the coast of Santa Catarina, and the interior of São Paulo and Minas Gerais. These communities seek to live in harmony with nature. But, without knowing it, they are contaminating the soil they step on and the water they drink.

### The Numbers of National Shame

Let's do the math. If only 30% of trained professionals regularly practice chemical treatment of bamboo, we have 450 active producing units in Brazil. Each one annually discards between 120 and 240 liters of effluent contaminated with boron compounds—totaling:

54,000 to 108,000 liters of toxic effluents dumped annually into Brazilian soil, carrying 2,700 to 5,400 kg of chemical compounds.

Of these, we estimate that 40% occur in environmentally sensitive areas: APPs, APAs, park buffer zones, and ecological tourism regions.

21,600 to 43,200 liters of effluents with 1,080 to 2,160 kg of boron compounds dumped annually in areas that should be protected.

And this is considering only artisanal treatment. We are not counting the disposal of treated poles that fail quality control (13% according to the UNESP study), nor contaminated solid waste (shavings, sawdust), nor the progressive leaching of boron over years in already saturated soils.

Boric acid, due to its very high solubility, is extremely mobile in the soil. In regions of high rainfall—such as the Atlantic Forest and the Amazon—up to 60% of the applied boron can leach, reaching groundwater and water bodies. Once in the aquatic environment, it causes toxicity to fish, aquatic plants, and invertebrates.

We are poisoning springs in the name of sustainability.

### The Criminal Negligence of Public Authorities

Meanwhile, what does the Brazilian government do?

* **Ministry of the Environment (MMA):** develops the National Bioeconomy Strategy, invests millions in the Amazon Bioeconomy and Innovation Park, prepares the triumphalist speech for COP30. And excludes bamboo from public policies.
* **ANVISA (National Health Surveillance Agency):** classifies CCA as "extremely toxic (Class I)" but allows its unrestricted commercialization for wood and bamboo treatment. It claims that inspection of use is the responsibility of IBAMA. A regulatory vacuum is created where no one inspects anything.
* **IBAMA:** requires registration of wood preservation industries, but small artisanal bamboo units operate completely outside regulation. No registration, no preservative consumption reports, no monitoring.
* **Law 12,484/2011 (National Policy to Encourage Sustainable Management and Cultivation of Bamboo):** has existed for 14 years. It was never regulated. Favored rural credit? Non-existent. Technical assistance? Non-existent. Certification? Non-existent. Commercial incentive? Non-existent.

Public authorities have abandoned Brazilian bamboo and, in doing so, have abandoned the sector's workers to their own devices.

This configures a chain of crimes by omission:

* **Failure to perform legal duty (Penal Code, art. 319)**
* **Administrative improbity (Law 8,429/1992)**
* **Health negligence that violates ANVISA's duty to "promote the protection of the population's health" (Law 9,782/1999)**
* **Environmental crime (Law 9,605/1998, art. 54): causing pollution that results or may result in damage to human health**

While the government prepares to sell the world the image of an Amazon managed with sustainable bioeconomy, workers in the Amazon, in the Atlantic Forest, and in the Cerrado are exposed daily to carcinogens. The hypocrisy is as large as the forest they claim to protect.

### The Ethical Duty of Public Retraction

And now we come to the most delicate point of this denunciation.

For years, I watched courses being taught where instructors assured students, with complete conviction, that treatment with boric acid was **"natural," "safe," "approved for decades."** I heard speakers state that **"boron is a plant micronutrient, therefore harmless."** I saw technical manuals that dedicated pages to the aesthetics of bamboo structures, but only a vague paragraph to **"individual protection."** These practices aggregate an unquestionable **MORAL** and **ETHICAL** obtuseness.

Was there bad faith? There was ignorance (or omission?). There was acritical repetition of old practices. There was, above all, the absence of dialogue with contemporary science.

But now, in October 2025, science can no longer be ignored. The UNESP/IFSP study, dedicated exclusively to Brazilian bamboo, with rigorous methodology (LCA according to ISO 14040 and ISO 14044), published in a peer-reviewed journal, is irrefutable evidence.

**93% Human Carcinogenic Toxicity.**

Given this, educational institutions, bioconstruction centers, sector associations, bamboo masters—all have an ethical, moral, and social responsibility duty to:

### 1. Publicly acknowledge the problem

Not with shame, but with maturity. Science evolves. Practices that seemed safe in the past prove problematic in the light of new evidence. Acknowledging this does not diminish the trajectory of those who dedicated decades to bamboo; on the contrary, it demonstrates a true commitment to sustainability.

### 2. Retract with former students

Thousands of people were trained in these techniques. Many may be, at this very moment, exposing themselves to risks without knowing. It is the moral responsibility of training institutions to warn them.

**To the Brazilian Bamboo Network (RBB), BambuBR, BambuSC, Goiás Bamboo Network, Bambuzal Bahia, Aprobambu:**

A public notice **PROMINENTLY DISPLAYED ON YOUR WEBSITES**, sent by email, posted on social media, published in newsletters, informing:

* Risks identified by the 2025 scientific study.
* The need to adopt safety protocols (PPE, ventilation, effluent treatment).
* The existence of non-toxic alternatives (thermal treatment, vegetable oils, physical methods).
* Offering update courses on safe practices.

### 3. Immediately review course curricula

It is no longer acceptable to teach treatment with boron compounds without:

* Clear and highlighted information about toxicological and carcinogenic risks.
* Mandatory occupational safety protocols.
* Effluent treatment systems (dumping into the soil is not an "option").
* Prioritizing non-toxic alternative methods.

### 4. Establish partnerships with universities and research institutes

**UNESP, IFSP, EMBRAPA, UFAC, UFG, UFPR**—all these institutions have the technical capacity to develop, test, and validate alternative treatment methods. The knowledge already exists in large part. What's missing is the bridge between academia and artisanal practice.

### 5. Create a National "Safe Bamboo" Campaign

The associations—**Brazilian Bamboo Network, BambuBR, BambuSC, Goiás Bamboo Network, Bambuzal Bahia, Aprobambu**—collectively represent the sector. Their institutional voice can promote changes on a national scale.

Educational materials, free webinars, voluntary certification of producers adopting safe practices, lobbying the MMA and ANVISA for effective regulation of Law 12,484/2011.

This is not a suggestion. It is an ethical imperative.

### The Unsustainable Contradiction of Bioconstruction

There is a painful irony in this story.

Bioconstruction was born as a resistance movement to the predatory model of conventional civil construction. Its founding principles—use of natural materials, minimal environmental impact, harmonic integration with the surroundings, respect for workers—are deeply ethical and necessary.

But by teaching techniques that expose workers to carcinogens and dump toxic effluents into APP springs, bioconstruction betrays its own principles. It becomes a form of educational *greenwashing*: selling the image of sustainability while practicing toxicity.

Ecovillages that take pride in living "in harmony with nature" may be, without knowing it, contaminating the soil they farm and the water their children drink. Permaculture centers that teach "care for the earth" may be poisoning springs that feed streams, rivers, and entire basins.

The inconsistency is intolerable.

And the most tragic part: alternatives exist. Thermal treatment (heating between 100–200°C with saturated steam) is effective and generates no toxic waste. Plant extracts have antifungal properties. Nanotechnology applied to bamboo—using nanoparticles extracted from the plant itself—has already shown antibacterial potential. Physical sap displacement methods (modified Boucherie method) are more efficient than prolonged dipping and use lower chemical concentrations.

The technology exists. The knowledge exists. Lack of willingness to change is what remains.

### The Outburst of One Who Was Ignored for a Decade

I need to be honest here.

For more than 10 years, I tried to engage. I presented data in university extension projects. I published technical articles warning about boron leaching in tropical soils. I proposed research projects on thermal treatment adapted to invasive exotic species. I sought partnerships with teaching centers to review their protocols.

I was treated as an alarmist. As an adventurer without academic credentials "disconnected from practical reality" (as if knowledge were the sole premise of universities and degree holders). I heard that "boric acid has been used for 50 years with no problem." That **"if it were so dangerous, it would have already killed someone."** That "there is no money for thermal treatment equipment, so this talk is elitist."

I watched, helpless, as classes of 20, 30, 40 students learned to dip bamboo in improvised tanks, drums, and pools of boric acid without gloves, without masks, without ventilation, 50 meters from a stream. And when I questioned, I was called "radical."

Today, science proved me right. But there is no satisfaction in that.

Because while I warned and was ignored, thousands of workers continued to be exposed. Tens of thousands of liters of toxic effluents continued to be dumped into APP soils. Springs continued to be contaminated.

How many cancer cases could have been avoided if we had acted 10 years ago?

That is the question that keeps me awake at night.

And because of that, today, I no longer accept silence. I no longer accept **"that's how it's always been done."** I no longer accept **"let's study the subject calmly."** Calm cost a decade of contamination.

### Science Can No Longer Be Ignored

The study by **Araújo, Gianelli, Mancini, and Medeiros**, published in April 2025, is the evidence that allows no further delay.

It is not a qualitative study based on perceptions. It is a quantitative Life Cycle Assessment, with primary data collection in a real production unit, analysis of impacts in 18 environmental categories, and statistical validation.

93% Human Carcinogenic Toxicity.

It's not 9%. It's not 30%. It's 93%.

This means that of all the environmental impact generated by artisanal bamboo production—including transport, tool use, energy consumption—the overwhelming majority comes from a single source: exposing human beings to substances that can cause cancer.

The study also reveals:

* 91% of the impact comes from contaminated waste (shavings, discarded poles).
* 13% of poles are rejected after treatment due to aesthetic or structural problems.
* 10% of water used in treatment is discarded directly into the soil.
* 25% of raw material is lost during processing.

It is an inefficient, predatory, and dangerous system.

And this is not an isolated problem in Acre. The study was conducted there, but the technique is taught nationwide. The problem belongs to all of Brazil.

### The Unpostponable Commitment

I write this article not as someone seeking revenge for being ignored. I write as someone who can no longer remain silent.

My commitment to the cause of sustainable bamboo is unwavering. I dedicated years of my life to this plant because I believe, deeply, in its potential to transform the Brazilian economy, protect forests, sequester carbon, and generate income for vulnerable communities.

Bamboo is extraordinary. It can grow up to 1 meter per day. It sequesters up to 80 tons of carbon per hectare. It regenerates degraded soils. It replaces wood, plastic, and concrete. It offers economic opportunities for family farmers in the Amazon, the Atlantic Forest, and the Cerrado.

But this transformation will only be real if it is safe. If it is **ethical**. If it is **truly sustainable.**

We cannot build the bioeconomy of the future on the contaminated bodies of poor workers. We cannot present bamboo as an environmental solution while poisoning springs.

Science, although ignored in years past, has now produced irrefutable evidence. And given that, only two options exist:

1. Act with responsibility, courage, and urgency—acknowledging the problem, retracting publicly, training workers, developing safe alternatives, and regulating the sector.

2. Ignore again, postpone, and minimize—and thus transform the Brazilian bamboo sector into an environmental and sanitary scandal that will be inevitably exposed, sooner or later.

I chose the first option more than 10 years ago. And I choose it again today.

I call upon all who love bamboo, who believe in true bioeconomy, who value the lives of workers and the integrity of ecosystems, to join me.

**Public retraction is not humiliation. It is responsibility.**

**Revision of practices is not weakness. It is evolution.**

**Dialogue with science is not elitism. It is survival.**
