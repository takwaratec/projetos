### The Treatments for Bamboo

Ecology and sustainability of processes, concepts, and practices.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*4-ssTKOdgZIEKqvpw_Bg7Q.jpeg)

In the countless recipes for treating and immunizing bamboo, water and fire are the main elements. The oldest reports say that leaving bamboo submerged in running water for 28 days after harvest can wash away much of the starch contained in the fibers. After being drained and as dry as possible, the bamboo was then smoked in improvised kilns by burning green branches of specific plants that produced preservative agents in the smoke.

In Asia, this process was done after the house was built, arranging a bundle of wood in the main room, which, after being lit, would burn for a week with the house completely closed, thus promoting the smoking of the entire environment. Many reports mention dipping or cooking bamboo in troughs with water and adding organic chemical agents obtained from barks, seeds, and leaves of plant species rich in flavoring, astringent, and antioxidant substances that promote protection against fungi and insects. Sea salt has been known for millennia for its preservative action on perishable foods and can also be used on bamboo; however, like all water-soluble salts, its action does not apply to extreme humidity conditions. The use of vegetable oils, waxes, and resins for waterproofing and reducing cracks are also well-known practices, and with so many varieties of plants cited in the literature, it can be said that the best recipe is the one where the ingredients are most easily available.

Depending on the intended use, bamboo does not need special care, as is the case with weaving mats, hampers, and baskets, where the outer part of the bamboo is used and the starch is removed by "snapping" the strips with a blade, separating the core from the skin. In general, it is possible to work with bamboo and obtain good preservation results without needing many resources; this was how civilizations developed until the present day. A recommendation that varies according to culture and region is cutting bamboo during the Waning Moon and in months without "R" (in Portuguese, May to August, the dry season in parts of Brazil), which is highly recommended here, but there is no scientific proof of this practice, evidencing only that in the dry season, bamboo becomes lighter due to water loss and in winter bamboo beetles go into hibernation. Some Chinese texts, for example, recommend cutting at dawn, before sunrise, right after the end of the rainy season, and, believe it or not, during the Full Moon.

Some bamboo species react well to direct fire treatment, resulting in a type of natural waterproofing glaze. Carbonization through the application of direct fire is a well-known technique in wood preservation and is still used today by remote populations, including for bamboo.

With scientific evolution, some processes began to be systematized, qualifying their effectiveness to standardize usage norms for commercial purposes. Extensive literature cites various techniques ranging from curing in the forest, immersion, cooking, and steaming to sap replacement through localized pressure or in steam autoclaves. The industrial revolution allowed the introduction of new chemical elements whose access was facilitated by the mechanization of ore extraction, producing raw material in large quantities, and these methods gradually became standard in high-scale industrial production environments.

The polymer industry introduced a huge range of adhesives, paints, and varnishes into the market, in addition to specific products for the immunization of vegetable fibers and aglomerates like wood or bamboo.

With the information age, many of these processes began to be questioned for causing considerable damage to the environment due to destructive extraction practices, as well as damage to the health of workers and users due to the toxicity found in these products.

Most developed countries established strict production norms and protocols, and in some cases, many of these products and processes were banned in some nations. Globalization allowed many of them to be transferred to developing countries like China to maintain production and supply the consumer market without causing damage to these "conscious" nations, transferring all risk to emerging peoples in the name of economic development. These products remained restricted to countries with more "liberal" legislation, such as Brazil. In this way, multinationals, most of which have their headquarters in ecological paradises, continue to profit from destructive practices in factories installed and consumers in the third world.

The 21st century dawned under the influence of ecological concepts and practices that, with the growing use of the term, began to trivialize the principles governing their correct application. It is very common to associate bamboo with an ecological and sustainable material due to its very nature, and consequently, everything made of bamboo acquired this connotation. Such behavior produced a dense fog that clouds discernment in correctly classifying a product as ecological and sustainable. Thus, the processes to which the product was subjected do not matter, whether in harvesting, treatment, finishes, or final use techniques in products or construction. It is urgent to combat this conceptual myopia if we want to advance with legitimately ecological and sustainable practices and norms.

Among the friendly treatments, Forest Curing would be the most accessible and simplified. After being cut, the bamboo should be left vertically with its leaves and branches for 30 days. The base of the culm should not have contact with the ground; it is recommended to support it on a stone or its own cut stump. The leaves continue to transpire water, decreasing the amount of sap, and the starch from the culms deposits at the foot, promoting fermentation. This treatment is also known as "avinagrado" (vinegary) in Colombia and repels beetles, but it is not effective against fungi.

Another modality of this curing is the immersion of the poles in a container with some antifungal mixture like tannin or tar (Bordeaux mixture); after the curing period, the tips are cut and the bamboo is placed upside down for another 15 days. This process does not guarantee penetration by capillarity throughout the length of the pole and may dye the bamboo. A similar system, DEV — Diffusion by Vertical Soaking, showed good efficiency and was developed by Bambu SC; consult the manual in the [repository](http://bambusc.org.br/?page_id=178).

Long live Bamboo! And long live all those who expose themselves to the treatment given to it.
