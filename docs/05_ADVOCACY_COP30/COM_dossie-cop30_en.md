## **1. Executive Summary**

The Regenerative Amazon Platform is an integrated proposal for ecological sanitation, ecological management of *Guadua spp.* bamboo, and the rapid deployment of low-carbon community bioindustries in the Legal Amazon, with a demonstration hub in Acre. The project brings together dry and riverside sanitation modules (BSM and BER, currently in the experimental stage), a biorefinery for activated carbon, biochar, and pyroligneous extract, lines of high energy density briquettes, and construction systems using bamboo-vegetable polyurethane biocomposites, operated within geodesic domes and community productive infrastructures.

The proposed solutions are supported by scientific evidence and related experiences in ecological sanitation, bioenergy, biopolymers, and sustainable architecture. However, they lack full validation in the integrated setup presented here, which is explicitly recognized as a technological innovation risk and opportunity. The Modular Dry Toilet (BSM) and Riverside Ecological Toilet (BER) technologies are supported by decentralized sanitation and social technology references, but in the formulation proposed by this project, they will initially be implemented as prototypes in a real-world environment, with monitoring protocols, iterative adjustments, and independent evaluation.

The Platform aligns with the missions of the *Nova Indústria Brasil* (New Brazil Industry), particularly mission 5 (bioeconomy, decarbonization, and energy transition) and mission 3 (infrastructure, sanitation, and housing). It also resonates with the guidelines of the Amazon Fund and the New Economy of the Amazon. The project will be presented comprehensively to Imperveg, a supplier of vegetable polyurethane tested in academic research and empirical prototypes, as a potential strategic partner in the bamboo biopolymers chain.

## **2. Matrix of Objectives, Products, and Results**

## **2.1 General Objective**

To implement and validate an integrated platform of ecological sanitation, ecological management of *Guadua spp.* bamboo, and low-carbon community bioindustries in the Legal Amazon, converting sanitation and forestry liabilities into productive, climate, and social assets, centered around cooperatives and women's organizations.

## **2.2 Specific Objectives**

1. To universalize access to ecological sanitation in selected floodplain and rural communities in the Amazon by implementing Modular Dry Toilet (BSM) and Riverside Ecological Toilet (BER) prototypes integrated into reverse logistics systems and Class A composting.
2. To structure a chain for the ecological management of *Guadua spp.* bamboo and agro-extractive residues (açaí, Brazil nut, babassu) to produce activated carbon, biochar, and high-density briquettes, replacing fossil sources and native firewood in thermal uses.
3. To establish community production lines for bamboo biocomposites with vegetable polyurethane and geodesic domes for controlled drying, resilient housing construction, and productive infrastructures, in technological partnership with suppliers like Imperveg.
4. To strengthen community governance and female-led bioeconomy networks through cooperatives, community Specific Purpose Entities (SPEs), and Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) processes.
5. To structure a replicable economic-financial model linking BNDES/Amazon Fund, Pronaf Bioeconomy, climate funds, and bioenergy and biochar carbon credit markets, with robust impact metrics.

## **3. Context, Structural Problems, and the Cost of Inaction**

## **3.1 Sanitation Deficit, DRSAI, and Climate Vulnerability**

The Legal Amazon combines low sanitation coverage, dispersed settlement patterns, and increasing climate pressure, which turns rivers and soils into vectors for waterborne diseases. In 2024, the lack of basic sanitation resulted in expenses of approximately R$ 174.3 million in hospitalizations due to inadequate water and sewage-related diseases in the Unified Health System (SUS), with a high incidence in the Northern and Amazonian regions.

Conventional sanitation technologies based on sewage networks and intensive water use prove technically and economically unfeasible in vast floodplain areas, stilt houses, and isolated communities, where the near-surface water table and seasonal floods make absorbent cesspools and centralized treatment plants unworkable. Climate change, in turn, intensifies extreme flood and drought events, escalating epidemiological risks and pressure on health and water supply systems.

## **3.2 The Paradox of Guadua spp. Bamboo and Fire Risk**

Amazonian states like Acre contain extensive formations of *Guadua spp.* bamboo, estimated at millions of hectares, possessing high potential for the generation of biomass, biochar, biopolymers, and structural materials, which is currently underutilized. The ecological dynamics of this species include synchronized death events in cycles of about three decades, producing a massive volume of dry biomass that increases susceptibility to forest fires and hinders the regeneration of tree species.

Without specific management and industrial utilization instruments, bamboo remains invisible in forestry policies while simultaneously contributing to fire scenarios and degradation that threaten biodiversity and climate stability. Converting this liability into a productive asset demands technological, regulatory, and market arrangements that the Platform aims to establish.

## **3.3 Fragmented Bioeconomy, Inequalities, and Gender**

Reports on the Brazilian and Amazonian bioeconomy indicate that most socio-biodiversity production still occurs within fragile structures, with low local value addition, reliance on intermediaries, and income concentration at the final stages of the supply chains. Research on community bioeconomy and women's networks shows that female labor is critical in gathering, processing, and management, but is rarely recognized in terms of decision-making power and access to productive resources.

Without structuring projects that connect social technologies, productive infrastructure, and appropriate financial instruments, there is a risk that "bioeconomy" solidifies as mere rhetoric disconnected from the reality of smallholder farmers and traditional peoples. The Platform aims to reduce this gap by organizing an industrial arrangement of a scale compatible with the territory but controlled locally.

## **4. Theory of Change and Strategic Alignment**

## **4.1 Central Hypothesis of the Intervention**

The central hypothesis of the Platform is that combining experimental ecological sanitation, ecological bamboo management, and community bioindustries—linked to a territorial governance emphasizing gender—can transform sanitary and forestry liabilities into economic and climate assets, generating long-term sustainable development.

By deploying dry and riverside sanitation module prototypes that prevent water contamination, the Platform aims to reduce the disease burden and free up resources currently spent on hospitalizations while simultaneously creating a stable flow of sanitary biomass for composting and agricultural use. By managing *Guadua spp.* and agro-extractive residues, the fire risk is diminished, native firewood and fossil fuels are substituted in thermal uses, and biochar is produced, holding the potential for generating permanent carbon credits.

By connecting these chains via geodesic domes and bamboo biopolymers with vegetable PU—co-developed with partners like Imperveg—distributed industrial capacity is created for resilient, low-carbon housing and infrastructure under community control. The project assumes that technological innovation coupled with financial instruments and inclusive governance can unlock pathways toward a standing-forest bioeconomy.

## **4.2 Alignment with BNDES, Amazon Fund, and New Brazil Industry**

The Platform aligns with:

* **Amazon Fund** – calls related to sanitation, forests, and bioeconomy, focusing on structuring and replicable projects in the Legal Amazon.
* **BNDES** – financing instruments for innovation, bioeconomy, and decarbonization, including lines from the *Nova Indústria Brasil* (TR, subventions, equity, government procurement).
* **New Economy of the Amazon (NEA)** – scenarios articulating land use, ecosystem services, and bioeconomy expansion compatible with 1.5°C climate goals.

The project contributes to the missions of *Nova Indústria Brasil* by promoting the decarbonization of thermal uses, the expansion of the technological use of biodiversity (bamboo, socio-biodiversity), innovative sanitation in rural contexts, and resilient residential infrastructure. Initial presentation to Imperveg reinforces the strategy to pair national tech suppliers with the Amazonian community productive base.

## **5. Component 1 – Experimental Ecological Sanitation and Biological Barriers**

## **5.1 BSM and BER as Technological Arrangements under Validation**

Component 1 is based on two solutions in the applied validation phase: the Modular Dry Toilet (BSM) and the Riverside Ecological Toilet (BER). The BSM was designed from consolidated ecological dry sanitation references—separating urine, adding carbonaceous material, and providing forced ventilation—and was recently presented to the fire brigade in Lençóis (Chapada Diamantina) as a pilot proposal, not yet completing a full cycle of installation and field monitoring.

The BER adapts the logic of ecological sanitation to the floodplain context, incorporating elevated structures, waterproof drums, and integration with rainwater harvesting. However, in the configuration proposed by the Platform, it is also considered an experimental setup to be tested at pilot scale. In both cases, the project explicitly acknowledges that these are socially inspired and technically grounded technologies, yet not operationally fully validated, proposing an associated action-research and epidemiological monitoring design.

## **5.2 Integration with Water and Activated Carbon Filters**

The sanitation modules will be combined with rainwater harvesting, storage, and treatment systems utilizing bamboo activated carbon filters, and when indicated, technologies such as Salta-Z and constructed wetlands. The activated carbon produced in Component 2 will be employed in domestic and community filters, optimizing water quality and creating a virtuous cycle linking sanitation, bioenergy, and water security.

## **5.3 Expected Impacts, Risks, and Mitigation**

In the pilot communities, a reduction in diffuse fecal contamination and indicators of waterborne diseases is expected throughout the validation cycle, monitored in partnership with local health services. The project acknowledges the technological risk associated with adopting not-yet-consolidated solutions and anticipates iterative adjustments to design, materials, and usage protocols based on the outcomes.

## **6. Component 2 – Ecological Bamboo Management and Carbon Biorefinery**

## **6.1 Guidelines for Ecological Management of Guadua spp.**

The project proposes management guidelines for *Guadua spp.* incorporating ecological zoning, extraction limits, maintenance of control plots, and biodiversity regeneration monitoring in dialogue with environmental agencies and regulatory frameworks. This management aims to minimize fire risk, free up space for arboreal regeneration, and supply the biorefinery with renewable biomass.

## **6.2 Charcoal, Biochar, and Pyroligneous Extract Biorefinery**

The community biorefinery will utilize kilns and boilers for the pyrolysis of bamboo and agro-extractive residues, producing charcoal, biochar, and pyroligneous extract. The activated carbon will be used for filters and industrial applications; biochar as a soil conditioner and a carbon credit asset; and pyroligneous extract as an input for biological control and ecological agriculture.

## **6.3 High-Density Briquettes and Fuel Substitution**

Solid residues will be converted into high-density briquettes suitable for regional boilers and thermal processes, decreasing the consumption of native firewood and fossil fuels. The design of the biomass workflow and machinery (splitters, briquette makers) is overseen by specialists in rural waste, ensuring adaptability to local conditions.

## **6.4 Biochar Carbon Credits and Recommendations**

Component 2 will align with biochar carbon credit methodologies, providing estimates of tCO₂e removed based on the stable biochar mass and its application in soils. However, we recommend a deeper exploration of financial modeling, MRV costs, benefit sharing, and market risks prior to final submission to carbon programs.

## **7. Component 3 – Bamboo Biopolymers, Geodesic Domes, and Resilient Construction**

## **7.1 Bamboo Biopolymers and Vegetable Polyurethane (Imperveg and similar)**

Component 3 investigates biocomposites comprising bamboo fibers and particles bonded by vegetable polyurethane (PU) derived from castor oil to produce panels, structural elements, and sealing components exhibiting good mechanical performance and moisture resistance. National studies show that bamboo-vegetable PU composites offer adequate flexural and compressive strength and dimensional stability for civil construction applications, provided appropriate curing processes and moisture parameters are followed.

Among potential vegetable PU suppliers, Imperveg stands out, whose products MAMONEX RD70, FL 133-A, and UG 132-A are referenced in technical bulletins and applied research as solvent-free two-component castor oil polyurethanes with robust adhesion and resistance to harsh environments. This report will be presented firsthand to Imperveg, reflecting their potential as a strategic and technological partner in validating processes for bamboo impregnation and structural panel manufacturing using vegetable PU.

## **7.2 Geodesic Domes as Greenhouses and Housing Structures**

Geodesic domes comprised of bamboo and biopolymers will serve as drying greenhouses, residential structures, and collective workspaces. Their geometry promotes natural ventilation, mitigates condensation points, and improves resilience to strong winds and heavy rainfall—features highly relevant for Amazonian environments. Integration with forced ventilation, dehumidification systems, and microclimatic monitoring will enhance control over the bamboo drying and biopolymer curing processes, reducing adhesion flaws and deformations.

## **7.3 Resilient Housing and LCA**

Combining domes and bamboo-vegetable PU panels seeks to yield social housing typologies and community infrastructure characterized by a low carbon footprint, solid thermal performance, and durability in humid climates. Life Cycle Analysis (LCA) and life cycle costing studies remain essential to comparatively quantify environmental, energy, and economic gains against traditional building systems.

## **8. Technical Team and Core Competencies**

## **8.1 Machinery, Rural Waste, and Bioenergy Core – Jesiel Campos**

Jesiel Campos focuses on the development of machinery to harness rural and extractive waste, designing small and medium-scale equipment tailored to the reality of the Amazon. His contributions include log splitters, tumbling machines, briquette makers, and processing solutions that make the bamboo and agro-extractive community biorefinery feasible.

## **8.2 Regenerative Technologies and Biopolymers Core – Fabio Takwara**

Fabio Takwara acts to conceptualize integrated regenerative technologies, including "Vegetable Solder" employing castor oil PU, geodesic domes, and bamboo drying and curing processes, interweaving traditional knowledge and materials engineering. His leadership anchors the creation of biopolymers and the utilization of domes as productive and housing infra-structures.

## **8.3 Sustainable Architecture Core – Tiago Filémon**

Tiago Filémon manages the architectural and construction systems, devising residential typologies, equipment, and productive spaces within bamboo domes and panels focused on thermal comfort, natural ventilation, and cultural alignment. This core works to confirm that the spatial models are socially appropriate and technically robust.

## **8.4 Bioeconomy, Gender, and Governance Core**

The bioeconomy and governance core weaves together academic knowledge and fieldwork in community bioeconomy, women's networks, and territorial development, manifesting them into participatory methodologies, FPIC procedures, institutional frameworks, and safeguard protocols. This nexus connects the project directly to state bioeconomy strategies, the New Economy of the Amazon, and regional gender and justice agendas.

## **9. Economic-Financial Model, Funding, and Replicability**

The Platform amalgamates revenues drawn from briquettes, activated carbon, biochar, bamboo-PU panels, geodesic domes, sanitation contracts, and biochar carbon credits. The financing roadmap projects merging non-reimbursable funds from the Amazon Fund with credit lines from BNDES and *Nova Indústria Brasil* instruments, beyond Pronaf Bioeconomy and specialized climate funds.

Replicability rests on modules adaptable to the ecological and social nuances of different Amazonian territories, supported by the shaping of regional hubs and dissemination via educational outreach and cooperative networks.

---

## **10. Remaining Gaps and Recommendations**

Principal gaps to address in complementary studies:

* Consolidation of validation protocols for BSM and BER prototypes featuring epidemiological and social acceptance metrics.
* Preparation of *Guadua spp.* management plans with monitoring encompassing biodiversity, regeneration, and fire risk.
* Thorough financial modeling of biochar carbon credits and related market risks.
* Deeper LCA and performance studies concerning geodesic domes and bamboo-vegetable PU panels, ideally with the technical engagement of partners like Imperveg.
* Formalization of an environmental, social, and gender safeguard framework concordant with Amazon Fund directives.
