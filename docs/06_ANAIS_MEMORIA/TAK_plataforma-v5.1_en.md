## 1. Executive Summary

The Regenerative Amazon Platform structures an integrated hub of low-carbon community bio-industries in the Legal Amazon, with a demonstrative hub in Acre. It articulates the ecological management of *Guadua* spp. bamboo, the utilization of agro-extractive residues, carbon biorefinery, and bamboo composites with vegetable polyurethane for resilient housing and productive infrastructure. *Guadua* spp. bamboo is the "wildcard" input of the Platform: in the base investment scenario, it alone is capable of feeding all essential biorefinery flows (briquettes, biochar, bamboo charcoal, and pyroligneous extract), providing thermally treated culms (“poison-free bamboo”) as a low-complexity primary product, and still supplying fibers and particles for the structural composites line.

The biorefinery is dimensioned to operate based on *Guadua* spp. and agro-extractive residues (açaí, chestnut, babassu), so that fluctuations in the supply or cost of complementary inputs—such as PET plastic residues, timber residues, or non-hazardous fractions of construction waste—never paralyze production; these flows enter as an upside, not as a condition for the feasibility of the industrial core. The management design incorporates Decentralized Primary Processing Units (UBPs) close to the harvest areas, dedicated to pre-processing (splitting, chipping, and pre-drying) to reduce volume and moisture of the biomass before transport, structurally addressing the Amazonian logistical cost and creating micro-hubs of work and income in the communities.

On this technological and logistical base, the base investment scenario focuses on the implementation of the carbon biorefinery, the UBPs, and the structuring of the ecological management chain of *Guadua* spp. and agro-extractive residues for the production of briquettes, biochar, bamboo charcoal, pyroligneous extract, and ecologically treated culms (“poison-free bamboo”), reaching economic-financial feasibility with revenues backed by already identified regional markets, without depending on sanitation contracts, the PET chain, high-purity activated carbon niches, or carbon credits.

In parallel, the Platform structures an ecological sanitation component (Modular Dry Toilet – BSM – and Riverine Ecological Toilet – BER) in a research-action regime, aimed at riverine and rural communities, with its own budget, team, and milestones; the integration of this sanitation as an anchor market for the industrial infrastructure—via service provision contracts, filters, and filtering media based on biochar and, when technically and regulatorily necessary, bamboo activated carbon produced from biochar, in short chains of water treatment and decentralized sanitation.

The bamboo–vegetable PU (castor oil) and Vegetable Welding route allows the manufacture of high-performance pre-cast elements—panels, bricks, tiles, beams, trusses, and columns—that follow to the construction site like a “bio-sovereignty Lego”: modular, lightweight, and quick-to-assemble pieces, capable of enabling social interest housing and community infrastructure in territories where conventional bio-construction with earth would be unfeasible due to climatic, logistical, or labor limitations. Priority finished products include: i) bamboo culms thermally treated with saturated steam enriched with pyroligneous extract (“poison-free bamboo”) for constructive, artisanal, and furniture uses; ii) bamboo briquettes and biochar for the replacement of native firewood and use as filtering media in low-complexity ecological sanitation arrangements; in applications requiring higher adsorption performance and regulatory compliance, biochar can be activated for the production of bamboo activated carbon in specific regional niches; iii) bamboo–vegetable PU biocomposite panels and bricks, suitable for ATHIS; iv) composite tiles made of cardboard/bamboo/sand with high thermal comfort; v) bamboo and composite geodesic domes for greenhouses, community centers, and resilient housing.

The Platform aligns with the missions of the *Nova Indústria Brasil*, the guidelines of the Amazon Fund, the Climate Fund – Green Industry, and the New Economy of the Amazon, incorporating evidence from Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) and recent academic validations on thermal/pyroligneous treatment technologies, bamboo–PU–PET composites, and BSM/BER ecological sanitation, granting technological maturity (TRL 8–9) to the proposed routes. The economic-financial model is structured in three scenarios (base, optimistic, and stress), with the biorefinery/bioenergy unit designed to reach the break-even point with only *Guadua* spp. and agro-extractive residues pre-processed in UBPs, while ecological sanitation, regional activated carbon niches, PET composites, and carbon credits are treated as impact and return accelerators, rather than a survival condition.

The combination of a resilient industrial core anchored in the wildcard bamboo, UBPs and optimized logistics, gradual financing tranches, and innovative components treated as R&D with risk management (ecological sanitation, PET chain, activated carbon niches, carbon credits), articulated with a Training Program for Leaders of the Amazonian Bioeconomy and a hybrid management model with “shadow management” and community succession goals, makes the Platform compatible with additionality criteria, robustness of impact, financial prudence, and socio-environmental safeguards of instruments such as BNDES/Amazon Fund, while maintaining the centrality of cooperatives and women's organizations in governance and value capture.

## 2. Matrix of Objectives, Products, and Results

### 2.1 General Objective

Implement and validate an integrated platform for ecological sanitation, ecological management of *Guadua* spp. bamboo, utilization of agro-extractive residues, and low-carbon community bio-industries in the Legal Amazon, converting sanitary, forest, and solid waste liabilities into productive, climate, and social assets, with centrality in cooperatives and women's organizations, in paid learning programs, and in territorial bamboo management plans articulated with Acre's scientific institutions, making Acre a reference state in ecological bamboo management and industrialization and a national hub for clean technology transfer.

### 2.2 Specific Objectives

1. Universalize access to ecological sanitation in selected riverine and rural communities through the implementation and validation of Modular Dry Toilet (BSM) and Riverine Ecological Toilet (BER) prototypes, integrated with reverse logistics, Class A composting, and epidemiological monitoring, treated as a research-action component with risk management.

2. Structure, in partnership with Acre's scientific institutions and environmental agencies, an ecological management chain for *Guadua* spp. bamboo that considers the mass mortality cycle, defines permanent areas of operation, and plans land and waterway transport routes, serving as a base for the progressive installation of new biorefineries around the bamboo patch in the state.

3. Implement community biorefineries for activated carbon, biochar, pyroligneous extract, and briquettes from *Guadua* spp. and agro-extractive residues (açaí, chestnut, babassu), replacing native firewood and fossil fuels in regional thermal uses, as the first replicable industrial core on the edge of the bamboo patch, with defined area of operation and transport routes set by the management committee in coordination with partner institutions, state government, and municipalities.

4. Implement community production lines for biocomposites based on bamboo and vegetable polyurethane (OSB-type panels, 3×3 m HIS sandwich panels, plates, bricks, and “Lego”-type structural pieces), operated in geodesic domes and other controlled drying solutions, for the construction of resilient housing and productive infrastructure in territories where conventional bio-construction in earth is unfeasible.

5. Develop and validate, at a pilot scale, reverse logistics arrangements and supply chains for post-consumer PET plastic residues, evaluating costs, volumes, and technical feasibility for use in bamboo–PU–PET composites, without PET availability being a condition for the operation of the biocomposites industrial core.

6. Strengthen community governance and women-led bioeconomy networks through cooperatives, community SPE structured as a holding for business units, and Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) processes, combining professional management in the initial phase with training programs and ‘shadow management’ for community succession, supported by paid learning scholarships from Phase 1.

7. Structure a modular and replicable economic-financial model, articulating BNDES/Amazon Fund, Climate Fund, Pronaf Bioeconomy, climate funds, and bioenergy and biochar carbon credit markets, with robust impact metrics and a base scenario independent of carbon credits, sanitation revenues, and the PET chain.

8. Reduce plastic pollution in Amazonian rivers and streams through the gradual and economically viable incorporation of post-consumer PET flows into high-value-added constructive composites, aligned with circular economy and environmental certifications, always starting from previously mapped and validated supply chains.

9. Structure a continuous program of paid technical courses and immersions, with limited spots for multipliers from other Amazonian regions and associated biomes, generating immediate revenue for the Platform and accelerating the replication of ecological sanitation, biorefinery, and biocomposite technologies.

## 3. Context, Structural Problems, and Cost of Inaction

### 3.1 Sanitary Deficit, DRSAI, and Climate Vulnerability

The Legal Amazon combines low sanitation coverage, dispersed occupation patterns, and increasing climate pressure, turning rivers and soils into vectors for waterborne diseases. In 2024, Brazil recorded about 344,400 hospitalizations for Diseases Related to Inadequate Environmental Sanitation (DRSAI), with an approximate cost of R$ 174.3 million to the SUS, with high incidence in the North region.

Conventional sanitation technologies based on sewage networks and intensive water use prove to be technically and economically unfeasible in vast areas of floodplain, stilts, and isolated communities, where rising water tables and seasonal floods make soak pits and centralized WTPs unfeasible. Climate change intensifies extreme flood and drought events, raising epidemiological risks and the pressure on health and supply systems, which reinforces the need for decentralized and adapted solutions such as BSM and BER.

### 3.2 The Paradox of Guadua spp. Bamboo and Fire Risk

Amazonian states like Acre concentrate extensive formations of *Guadua* spp. bamboo, estimated at about 4.5 million hectares, with high potential for biomass, biochar, biopolymers, and structural materials, which is currently underutilized. Species such as *Guadua weberbaueri* and *G. sarcocarpa* have a semelparous cycle, with synchronized death every 28–30 years, generating a "pulse" of dry fuel that drastically increases the risk of catastrophic fires and hinders the regeneration of tree species.

Recent studies on the ecology of these *Guadua* forests in Acre, conducted by Sonaira Souza da Silva, quantify the increase in fuel load and its effects on the regeneration of tree species, reinforcing the need for active management as a mitigation measure for fire and restoration. Without specific instruments for management and industrial use, bamboo remains invisible in forest policies, while contributing to fire and degradation scenarios that threaten biodiversity, climate stability, and the security of traditional populations.

In addition to fire risk, the current processing chain for bamboo and wood in Brazil is dominated by preservatives such as CCA (chromated copper arsenate), CCB (chromated copper borate), copper salts, and boron solutions, widely used as ‘popular treatment’ for eucalyptus and bamboo, without systematic traceability or consolidated LCA studies. A Life Cycle Assessment conducted by Araújo et al. in production units in Acre demonstrated that Human Carcinogenic Toxicity represents 93% of the total impact of the boron-treated bamboo chain, with about 91% of this impact associated with inadequate management of liquid effluents and contaminated solid waste. The leaching of these preservatives, especially boron, in open-air treatment yards, represents a direct vector of contamination of soils, rivers, and groundwater, with potential contribution to fish mortality and impacts on local food chains, although bamboo treatments have not yet been the subject of specific ecotoxicology studies on a national scale.

The permanence of the use of CCA/CCB and boron treatments without effective control by regulatory agencies creates a ‘toxic bioeconomy’ scenario, incompatible with environmental and sanitary safeguards and the eligibility criteria of instruments such as the Amazon Fund and the Climate Fund, and reinforces the urgency of low-impact thermal and biopolymeric routes such as those proposed by the Platform.

### 3.3 Fragmented Bioeconomy, Inequalities, and Gender

Reports on the Brazilian and Amazonian bioeconomy indicate that most socio-biodiversity production still occurs in fragile structures, with low local value aggregation, dependence on intermediaries, and income concentration in the final stages of the chains. Research in community bioeconomy and women's networks shows that female work is fundamental in collection, processing, and management, but rarely recognized in terms of decision-making power and access to productive assets and industrial infrastructure.

### 3.4 Plastic Crisis in the Amazon

Recent studies and reviews point to the Amazon basin as one of the most plastic-contaminated in the world: the Amazon river already figures among the largest emitters of plastic waste into the ocean, with estimates of hundreds of thousands of annual tons discarded in the Amazonian environment. Microplastics—largely associated with polymers such as PET—have already been detected in water, sediments, and fish consumed by riverine populations, configuring an integrated One Health risk that adds to the sanitation deficit described above.

### 3.5 Housing Deficit, Inadequate Dwellings, and Unemployment

In the housing dimension, Acre presents an estimated deficit of about 25–29 thousand households, with more than 15 thousand dwellings considered precarious, according to data from the João Pinheiro Foundation and recent studies on state reality. In the Legal Amazon, approximately 20.8% of households present inadequate housing conditions, and Acre figures among the states with higher relative percentages of inadequacy, with about 24.4% of dwellings in inadequate conditions.

In the labor market, the unemployment rate in Acre remained around 7–7.5% in 2023–2024, affecting tens of thousands of people, with high underutilization of the workforce and strong presence of informal occupation. This scenario combines housing deficit, urban precariousness, and structural unemployment, indicating that sanitation, social interest housing, and community-based bio-industries policies need to be articulated to simultaneously generate dignified housing, work, and income in Amazonian territories.

## 4. Theory of Change and Strategic Alignment

### 4.1 Central Intervention Hypothesis

The central hypothesis is that a low-risk community industrial core, anchored in ecological management of *Guadua* spp. bamboo and biomass biorefineries, combined with ecological sanitation in a research-action regime, reverse logistics of PET treated as a pilot, and bamboo–vegetable PU composites for housing and infrastructure, articulated with territorial governance with a focus on gender and paid training of managers, can transform sanitary, forest, and solid waste liabilities into economic, climate, and social assets. Thus, sanitation, PET, and carbon credits act as progressive layers of innovation and “upside” over an economic **core** based on wildcard bamboo and agro-extractive residues, which must remain viable even in conservative market scenarios.

To dialogue with the risk profile of BNDES, the Amazon Fund, and other financers, the theory of change unfolds into three financial scenarios integrated into the business model: (i) a base or “realistic pessimistic” scenario, in which the biorefinery operates only with “bread and butter” products—high-density briquettes for regional thermal uses, biochar for local uses and future carbon credits, bamboo charcoal, and pyroligneous extract, addition to ecologically treated bamboo—sold at conservative prices in existing markets; (ii) an optimistic scenario, in which innovation pilots—ecological sanitation, filters and filtering media from bamboo charcoal and biochar for water and effluents, composites with PET, biochar carbon credits—are consolidated, accelerating the return on investment; and (iii) a stress scenario, which combines the non-scaling of pilots with external shocks (such as increased logistical costs), in which the industrial core still remains operational, although with lower profitability.

In this design, bamboo activated carbon is not treated as a commodity destined to compete with large global activated carbon players, but as a niche product for short water treatment and decentralized sanitation chains in the Amazon, conditioned on the existence of specific contracts with municipalities, rural water programs, community filters, and BSM/BER arrangements, and not as an “automatic plan B” in case of frustration of the sanitation component.

### 4.2 Alignment with BNDES, Amazon Fund, Climate Fund, and New Industry Brazil

The Platform integrates with the Amazon Fund calls (sanitation, forests, bioeconomy), the Climate Fund – Green Industry lines, the BNDES instruments in innovation and bioeconomy, and the missions of the *Nova Indústria Brasil 2024–2033* (decarbonization of thermal uses, sanitation, housing, bioeconomy), by structuring a green industrial core (biorefineries and biocomposites), an ecological sanitation component in research-action, and a robust community governance and training program focused on women and youth. The explicitation of financial scenarios, a structured community succession program, and a logistical plan with decentralized primary processing (UBPs) reinforces the adherence of the Platform to additionality, financial prudence, and risk management criteria of these instruments.

### 4.3 Economic Flywheel of the Regenerative Amazon Platform

The investment thesis is explicitly stated as an economic flywheel, organized around a resilient industrial engine, with impact accelerators:

1. **Engine – Biorefinery and Wildcard Bamboo**: the carbon biorefinery and the production of briquettes, biochar, bamboo charcoal, pyroligneous extract, and ecologically treated culms (“poison-free bamboo”) from *Guadua* spp. and agro-extractive residues constitute the lowest-risk economic core, capable of reaching operational break-even with only bamboo and residues of açaí, chestnut, and babassu, independently of the entry of PET, carbon credits, ecological sanitation, or high-purity activated carbon. This engine is dimensioned to remain viable in the base scenario, with conservative prices and regional logistics, functioning as a replicable “reference plant” in new Amazonian territories.

2. **Productive Layer – Biocomposites and Resilient Construction**: on this core, the biocomposites line based on bamboo and vegetable PU (OSB-type panels, 3×3 m HIS sandwich panels, plates, bricks, and “Lego”-type structural pieces), operated in geodesic domes and other controlled drying solutions, expands the portfolio for social interest housing and productive infrastructure in territories where bio-construction with earth is unfeasible. The incorporation of post-consumer PET into bamboo–PU–PET composites is treated as a complementary route, conditioned on the validation of supply chains, logistical costs, and results of grinding and plasticization pilots, so that PET unavailability does not affect the feasibility of the biocomposites core.

3. **Social Trigger – Ecological Sanitation and Safe Water**: demand for sanitation and drinking water is addressed through BSM and BER prototypes in research-action, with their own resources, goals, and indicators, generating, when validated, contracts, recurring revenues, and stable demand for briquettes, filters, and filtering media based on bamboo charcoal and biochar. These ecological sanitation arrangements are treated as innovation components with risk management: they operate as a “social trigger” and potential anchor market only if they reach pre-defined epidemiological, adherence, and cost-effectiveness milestones, without the viability of the industrial core depending previously on these contracts.

4. **Accelerators – Carbon Credits, Niche Activated Carbon, LCA, and Certifications**: biochar opens space for removal credits; the replacement of boron/CCA by thermal-pyroligneous routes drastically reduces Human Carcinogenic Toxicity, as demonstrated by Araújo et al.; and the researches of Carvalho, Cazella, Lopes, and others demonstrate mechanical performance and stability of bamboo–PU–PET composites comparable or superior to commercial OSB, enabling LEED, AQUA-HQE, and Blue House Seal certifications. Activated bamboo carbon, in turn, is explored as a niche accelerator, aimed at specific water treatment and decentralized sanitation contracts in the Amazon, and not as a commodity destined to compete with large global manufacturers—its contribution to NPV and IRR is considered only in optimistic and expansion scenarios, and always associated with short chains and previously mapped contracts.

5. **Expansion – Territorial Scale, UBPs, and Replication**: accumulated revenues and capacities allow modular replication in new Amazonian territories, with networked regional hubs based on new biorefineries on the edge of the *Guadua* patch, domes/greenhouses, UBPs near management areas, and composite lines, anchored in territorial management plans agreed with governments and scientific institutions. Decentralized primary processing—splitting, chipping, and pre-drying of bamboo in community UBPs—reduces weight and volume transported (“transporting the juice, not the whole orange”), directly addresses the structural logistical cost, and creates local micro-hubs of work and income.
