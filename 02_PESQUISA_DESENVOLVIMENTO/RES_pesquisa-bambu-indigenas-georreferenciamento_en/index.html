    Integrated Analysis of Bamboo Forests in Acre: Ecological Dynamics, Geoprocessing Mapping, and Socio-environmental Governance among IFAC, UFAC, and IMAC

    The southwestern region of the Amazon, with a special emphasis on the state of Acre, constitutes one of the most unique ecosystems within the Amazon biome due to the vast predominance of open forests featuring arborescent bamboos of the Guadua genus. These formations, locally known as tabocais, not only shape the physiognomy of approximately 38% of the state's forest cover but also impose unique challenges and opportunities for forest management, biodiversity conservation, and the sustainability of traditional and indigenous populations. The complexity of this biome demands a transdisciplinary approach that unites the rigor of botanical taxonomy, the precision of geotechnologies, and the robustness of public policies for licensing and territorial management. In this scenario, the institutional triangle formed by the Federal University of Acre (UFAC), the Federal Institute of Acre (IFAC), and the Acre Environmental Institute (IMAC) emerges as the core engine for research, innovation, and environmental regulation.

    The bamboo forests of Acre are characterized by a dynamic mosaic where bamboo, especially the species Guadua weberbaueri and Guadua sarcocarpa, acts as a landscape-structuring agent, influencing the birth and death rates of surrounding tree communities. Understanding these dynamics is fundamental for mapping and quantification projects, as the biomass of these areas differs significantly from dense forests, presenting substantial variations compared to palm-dominated areas. This biomass variation has direct implications for the regional carbon balance and for bioeconomy strategies that aim to value bamboo as a high value-added renewable resource.

    The Role of Institutions and Actor Articulation in Acre

    Natural resource governance in Acre is marked by strong collaboration between the academic sector and public authorities. Identifying the actors involved in bamboo mapping and management reveals a sophisticated network of researchers, managers, and technicians operating at different scales.

    Federal University of Acre (UFAC): Botanical Research and Remote Sensing

    UFAC has established itself as the main pole of biological knowledge on bamboo in the Southwestern Amazon. Through its Zoobotanical Park and Herbarium (HPZ), the institution has cataloged species diversity and studied successional dynamics in experimental areas such as the Catuaba Experimental Farm and the Humaitá Forest Reserve. Researchers have led studies integrating botanical systematics with landscape ecology, providing the necessary scientific basis for forest inventories and management plans.

    UFAC's expertise extends to geoprocessing, where graduate programs develop advanced spectral classification methodologies for mapping bamboo forests. The use of algorithms such as Maximum Likelihood (MAXVER) and Minimum Distance (MINDIS) has allowed accurate separation of vegetation classes in satellite images, overcoming the difficulties imposed by the dense bamboo-dominated understory.

    Federal Institute of Acre (IFAC): Applied Technology and Rural Extension

    IFAC plays a crucial role in transforming scientific knowledge into technological applications and extension services for communities. At the Sena Madureira campus, students and researchers have developed biodegradable nanotechnological bandages from bamboo cellulose, demonstrating innovation potential for the biomedical industry. In the Juruá Valley, the Cruzeiro do Sul campus focuses on valorizing socio-biodiversity products, integrating research into the reality of local populations and promoting the exchange of experiences on bioeconomy, including international missions.

    IFAC's reach is characterized by its capillarity, extending to municipalities like Xapuri and Cruzeiro do Sul, where native bamboo forests are predominant. The institution works to strengthen collaborative networks involving rural producers and cooperatives, seeking sustainable economic alternatives that minimize deforestation and utilize available biomass.

    Acre Environmental Institute (IMAC): Regulation and Standardization

    IMAC is the regulatory arm ensuring that bamboo exploitation occurs within the limits of legality and sustainability. IMAC Normative Instruction No. 10, of October 14, 2015, is the guiding document that established the Terms of Reference for the environmental licensing of bamboo exploration and management in the state. This legal framework is fundamental to the project, as it defines the technical criteria, necessary documentation, and procedures for tracking the origin of forest products.

    Ecology and Taxonomy of the Guadua Genus in Acre

    Understanding the Guadua genus is central to any mapping and quantification effort. In Acre, the most frequent species with the highest ecological impact are Guadua weberbaueri Pilger and Guadua sarcocarpa Londoño & Peterson. Both exhibit a broad distribution, frequently occurring on tabular interfluves over dystrophic soils. The taxonomy of these plants is complex due to their long reproductive cycles and gregarious flowering, which can occur at intervals of 25 to 30 years.

    Characteristics of Key Species

    Species of the Guadua genus in Acre possess morphological traits that facilitate field identification, although remote mapping requires more refined techniques.

    • Guadua weberbaueri: Characterized by dense and arched culms, dominating vast clearing areas. Its mass die-off after flowering opens space for successional processes.
    • Guadua sarcocarpa: Often associated with G. weberbaueri, it exerts a strong influence on the mortality of neighboring trees due to the weight of its canopy and competition for light in the understory.
    • Guadua superba: Occurs in more restricted areas, frequently linked to temporarily flooded soils or low plateaus.

    Biomass and Carbon Quantification

    Quantifying bamboo biomass is a critical component for REDD+ projects and environmental conservation. Studies indicate that in typologies dominated by an understory of bamboo, the bamboo can represent an increment of over 10% in the total forest biomass.

    Forest Typology Canopy Characteristics Tree Regeneration
    Forest with Bamboo Open, dense understory Limited by competition with bamboo
    Forest with Palms Closed, presence of middle strata High biodiversity of seedlings
    Dense Forest Closed, high basal area Natural gap dynamics

    It is estimated that 40% of the deforested area in Acre over the last 10 years consisted of forests with bamboo. Accurate mapping and the economic valorization of bamboo can reverse this trend, offering a sustainable exploration alternative.

    Mapping and Geoprocessing Technologies

    Mapping bamboo forests in Acre utilizes advanced remote sensing tools and Geographic Information Systems (GIS). The technical challenge lies in the spectral distinction between bamboo foliage and canopy trees, especially in medium-resolution imagery.

    Classification Algorithms and Results

    Research conducted within UFAC's graduate programs tested various supervised classification algorithms on satellite scenes. The best overall accuracy results were obtained by the Spectral Angle Mapper (SAM) method, achieving 95% precision. Other methods, such as Maximum Likelihood (MAXVER) and Minimum Distance (MINDIS), also presented robust results.

    Georeferencing and Ecological-Economic Zoning (ZEE)

    The state of Acre possesses a solid history of territorial planning through its Ecological-Economic Zoning (ZEE/AC). Integrating bamboo occurrence data into the ZEE makes it possible to identify where traditional populations can engage in sustainable management without compromising the integrity of Conservation Units or Indigenous Lands.

    Indigenous Communities and Territorial Management

    Acre is home to remarkable ethnic diversity, with many indigenous lands located in areas of high bamboo prevalence, especially in the Juruá Valley. The relationship between indigenous peoples and bamboo forests is intrinsic; bamboo is utilized for manufacturing tools, musical instruments, traditional construction, and rituals.

    Indigenous Actors and Organizations

    Territorial management in these areas is sustained through a complex network of organizations and associations. The Pro-Indian Commission of Acre (CPI-Acre) has been a fundamental partner in training Indigenous Agroforestry Agents (AAFIs), who work in environmental monitoring, surveillance, and management of their lands.

    The Juruá Valley and Indigenous Lands near Bamboo Groves

    In the state's extreme west, the Upper Juruá region harbors ethnicities such as the Nukini, Nawa, Puyanawa, and Katukina. Management of these lands is guided by Territorial and Environmental Management Plans (PGTAs), which utilize ethnomapping to identify available natural resources and plan their use.

    Indigenous Agroforestry Agents (AAFIs) and Monitoring

    AAFIs merge traditional knowledge with modern management techniques, adopting responsibility for implementing agroforestry systems and monitoring nurseries and management areas. This shared management model is essential for bamboo mapping and quantification projects to attain legitimacy and efficacy within indigenous lands.

    Legislation and Sustainability of Bamboo Management

    Bamboo exploration in Acre is governed by a regulatory framework that seeks to balance economic development with environmental protection, grounded on IMAC Normative Instruction No. 10/2015.

    Bamboo as a Development Alternative

    Native bamboo is seen as a strategic alternative for the socio-economic development of Acre. Technological development, allied with biomass monitoring, provides practical inputs for REDD+ projects and long-term conservation.

    Scientific Conclusion

    The collaboration network among academia (UFAC and IFAC), the State (IMAC and SEMA), social organizations (CPI-Acre and AMAAIAC), indigenous leadership, and the technological research sector is the essence of a robust management model. Quantifying the biomass of Guadua precisely, supported by satellite mapping and traditional agroforestry knowledge, converts the Acrean bamboo forest from a mere vegetational component into a structural pillar of the Southwestern Amazon's sustainable bioeconomy.
