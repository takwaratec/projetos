## Abstract

This bulletin analyzes the application of geodesic domes as critical infrastructure for microclimatic control in the Amazon, focusing on overcoming the humidity bottleneck for the sustainable industrialization of native bamboo (*Guadua* spp.). Thermodynamic principles of the chimney effect, structural efficiency of spherical geometry, and integration with Takwara Technology (Vegetable Solder with Castor Polyurethane) are discussed. The document validates the geodesic dome not just as a greenhouse, but as a decentralized processing center and catalyst for regional bio-sovereignty, presenting performance indicators, condensation mitigation, and alignment with funding frameworks (BNDES).

**Keywords:** geodesic domes · microclimate · bio-sovereignty · Guadua · Amazon · Takwara Technology.


## **1. The Paradigm of Bio-sovereignty and the Amazon Challenge**

National Bio-sovereignty is defined by a country's or region's ability to exercise technical and economic control over its biological resources, transforming them into industrial assets without compromising the ecological integrity of the biome.1 In the Amazon, this concept is manifested in the "Bamboo Paradox": although Acre has an estimated reserve worth US$ 15.2 billion, the material remains underutilized due to operational paralysis and informality, which reaches 95% of current production.1

### **1.1. The Bamboo Death Cycle and Ecological Security**

Industrial management of native bamboo is not just an economic opportunity but a necessity for environmental survival.1 The species *Guadua spp.* presents a peculiar life cycle, with synchronized death every 28 to 30 years. This phenomenon generates a massive accumulation of dry fuel on the forest floor, creating ideal conditions for catastrophic forest fires that can decimate native trees and compromise local biodiversity.1 By promoting controlled extraction and processing, the Bio-sovereignty project acts in disaster prevention and the restoration of natural regeneration.1

### **1.2. Humidity as a Limiting Factor for Industrialization**

For bamboo to be used as a high-performance engineering material (with compressive strength in the 48-60 MPa range), it must be integrated with biopolymers, such as polyurethane (PU) derived from castor oil.1 However, the successful application of this "Vegetable Solder" requires that the internal humidity of the bamboo culm be reduced to a maximum of 12%.1 In an environment where external humidity is omnipresent, natural drying is insufficient and vulnerable to attacks by fungi and borers that thrive in stagnant, humid air conditions.5

## **2. Geodesic Dome Engineering as a Microclimatic Solution**

Geodesic domes offer superior structural and thermodynamic responses compared to traditional horizontal greenhouses.1 Based on spherical geometry and the interconnection of equilateral triangles, these structures ensure maximum free spans with minimum material, facilitating the air circulation necessary for humidity control.1

### **2.1. Passive Ventilation Dynamics and the Chimney Effect**

Unlike conventional greenhouses, where ventilation is limited to the sides, the spherical shape of the geodesic dome induces a naturally circular air flow.1 The central phenomenon is the "natural chimney effect" (or *stack effect*), enhanced by the height of the structure relative to its base area.7 Air heated by solar radiation or internal biorefinery processes becomes less dense and rises toward the apex (highest point), where natural exhausters or strategic openings allow its exit.1 This movement creates a negative pressure zone at the base, which sucks fresh, dry air (if properly filtered or treated) into the interior.7

The mathematical modeling of this flow is expressed by the thermal buoyancy ventilation equation:

**Q = Cd * A * √(2 * g * h * (Ti - Te) / Ti)**

Where:
* **Q** represents the volumetric flow rate.7
* **A** is the area of the ventilation openings.7
* **h** is the vertical height between the air inlets at the base and the outlet at the top.7
* **Ti** and **Te** are the internal and external temperatures in Kelvin.7

In a 25-meter diameter geodesic dome (as proposed for excellence centers), the 12.5-meter height (h) generates a ventilation driving force significantly superior to any conventional industrial shed, allowing for complete air renewal rates every 1 or 2 minutes.1 This rapid renewal is what prevents moisture generated by plant transpiration or biomass drying from condensing on internal surfaces, a chronic problem in Amazonian constructions.7

### **2.2. Surface Efficiency and Thermal Control**

The geodesic dome has approximately 30% less external surface area than a parallelepiped of the same volume.1 This characteristic reduces unwanted heat transfer from the outside to the inside, maintaining more stable temperatures.1 In equatorial climates, where solar radiation is intense, the aerodynamic shape of the dome allows wind to flow smoothly over the surface, dissipating heat and reducing structural stress that often damages plastic coverings of common greenhouses.1

## **3. Takwara Technology and Vegetable Solder: The Heart of Bio-sovereignty**

The validation of geodesic dome use in the Amazon is inseparable from the innovation brought by Takwara Technology.1 Developed by Fabio Takwara, this social technology integrates traditional knowledge with biopolymer science to create fast, low-impact construction systems.1

### **3.1. Use of Castor Oil Polyurethane (PU)**

The "Vegetable Solder" is based on two-component polyurethane derived from castor oil (such as IMPERVEG FL 133 A).1 This material possesses unique properties that make it ideal for the Amazonian environment after controlled drying in the dome:

* **Deep Adhesion:** The resin penetrates bamboo, concrete, or brick pores, ensuring maximum fixation that prevents future moisture passage.13
* **Dimensional Stability:** Being 100% solid and solvent-free, the product does not shrink after curing.13 In the case of bamboo, this means pieces remain stable even with variations in external humidity.1
* **Environmental Resistance:** After total polymerization (6 hours), the membrane is resistant to UV rays, acids, alkalis, and abrasion.13
* **Hydric Security:** It is approved for contact with drinking water, ensuring the structure does not contaminate the surrounding ecosystem.13

### **3.2. Decentralized Industrialization and Free Social Technologies**

The project envisages the use of machines customized by the Jesiel/Takwara Nucleus, such as the tumbling machine and the star-blade splitter, which operate at a community scale.1 The transition to bio-sovereignty occurs when local communities, organized in cooperatives (with a focus on female management), master these thermal and chemical processing procedures.1 The use of saturated steam inside geodesic greenhouses eliminates starch from the bamboo, preventing pests without the need for toxic chemical preservatives like Boron or Copper Chromated Arsenate (CCA), which account for 93% of traditional chain toxicity.1

## **5. Humidity and Condensation Mitigation: Advanced Strategies**

To ensure internal relative humidity remains below the critical threshold of 60% (necessary for bamboo stability at 12%), the project adopts active and passive microclimatic management techniques.5

### **5.1. Combating Nighttime Condensation**

The main challenge in the Amazon is nighttime cooling, which raises relative humidity and causes condensation on cold surfaces.9 In geodesic domes, the absence of flat ceilings eliminates dripping on the material, as condensation runs down the curvature to the base, where it can be drained.7 Additionally, the "Night Purge" strategy consists of keeping the apex exhauster open, allowing heat accumulated during the day to escape slowly, maintaining air circulation even in the absence of wind.7

### **5.2. Integration with Dehumidification Technologies**

In critical phases of bamboo drying or processing sensitive products (such as high-quality cocoa), the dome can be integrated with:

* **Solid Desiccant Systems:** Use of silica packs or charcoal briquettes (produced from bamboo waste) to absorb residual moisture during periods of intense rain.25
* **Solar Thermal Heating:** Utilization of the geodesic structure itself to capture infrared radiation, raising the internal temperature slightly above the external to force accelerated evapotranspiration.11
* **Smart Sensors:** IoT sensor monitoring of the Dew Point, activating mechanical ventilation systems only when condensation risk is detected.5

## **6. Sustainability and Social Interest Housing (ATHIS)**

Humidity control in geodesic domes has a direct impact on the health and quality of life of local populations through Social Interest Housing (ATHIS).1 Traditional constructions in the Amazon suffer from mold, mildew, and early wood decay due to poor ventilation.6

Bio-sovereignty platform domes offer:

* **Indoor Air Quality:** CO2 levels maintained in healthy ranges (600-800 ppm) due to continuous air exchange, reducing respiratory problems.7
* **Thermal Comfort:** Internal temperature reduction by up to 4 to 6 Kelvin without air conditioning, decreasing inhabitants' thermal stress.7
* **Climate Resilience:** Ability to withstand extreme weather events, such as storms and strong winds, which are becoming more frequent with deforestation and global changes.1

## **7. Conclusions and Strategic Recommendations**

The presented technical analysis unequivocally validates that the use of geodesic domes as greenhouses is the most efficient ally to overcome the challenge of humidity control in the Amazon, a *sine qua non* condition for the success of the National Bio-sovereignty proposal.\[1, 1\]

Evidence demonstrates that geodesic geometry, when integrated with Takwara Technology and the use of biopolymers like castor oil Vegetable Solder, creates an autonomous, replicable, and low-cost industrial infrastructure.1 This model not only makes the bamboo economy viable but also enhances socio-biodiversity value chains (açaí, cocoa, bio-inputs) through high-precision processing.19
