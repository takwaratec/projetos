## Abstract

This bulletin describes the integrated micro-biorefinery of the Regenerative Amazon Platform, a high-performance modular thermochemical system designed for the decentralized conversion of native bamboo (*Guadua* spp.) into bioeconomy assets. The system combines slow pyrolysis, thermal cascading drying, and non-toxic phytosanitary treatment via enriched pyroligneous extract. Engineering safety axes (as per NR-13), neutral-flow thermodynamic balance for emission mitigation, and the TRL 4-7 scalable roadmap are discussed, positioning biochar and 2nd generation fuels as pillars of energy and climate sovereignty in the Legal Amazon.

**Keywords:** community biorefinery · pyrolysis · biochar · Guadua · energy sovereignty · thermal cascading.

## 2. Thematic Axis and Base Engineering

### Axis I — Biomass Characterization and Properties

Brazil is the largest repository of bamboo diversity in the Americas. The project uses specific yield data from the national territory:

**Guadua spp. (Amazon / Acre)**
The Southwestern Amazon is home to the world's largest native bamboo forest. In Acre alone, there are an estimated 4.5 to 7 million hectares dominated by *Guadua weberbaueri* (taboca) and *Guadua sarcocarpa*, totaling an estimated 21.8 billion culms. Although *G. angustifolia* presents better structural indices for construction, the vast dominance of *G. weberbaueri* in the Acre territory positions it as the primary volume raw material for chemical conversion into biochar, bioplastics, and pyrolysis.

**Phyllostachys aurea and P. nigra (South and Southeast)**
Fast-growing species (27.2 t/ha.year), with excellent gravimetric yield for pyroligneous extract and high energy quality for charcoal production.

---

### Axis II — Thermochemical Process and Co-products

#### The Role of Pyroligneous Extract

The system operates at a final temperature between 300 °C and 600 °C. The average biochar yield is in the range of 28% to 32% of dry mass, while crude pyroligneous extract (PE) varies from 40% to 45%. Strict thermal control ensures the absence of toxic Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs) in the collected condensed fraction.

**Circular Integration of Pyroligneous Extract**
PE is not just a commercial byproduct (agricultural pesticide). Rich in phenols and organic acids, it is added at a 20% ratio to the boiler feed water (NR-13), generating chemically enriched saturated steam. This steam impregnated with phenols from the bamboo itself acts as a structural preservative with fungicidal and bactericidal action — closing a circular material treatment cycle without external inputs.

---

### Axis III — Equipment Engineering and Industrial Safety

Safety is the watershed of the operation. The system adopts the following standards as non-negotiable minimum requirements:

**NR-13 (Pressure Vessels and Boilers)**
The steam generator boiler for hydrothermal and chemical bamboo treatment has independent mechanical calculation, safety valves calibrated to the Maximum Allowable Working Pressure (MAWP), and mandatory periodic technical inspection.

**Industrial Prototyping Architecture (Technical Rigor 2.0)**
The system has evolved to a high-precision cylindrical processing model, optimizing cubage for whole culm handling:
- **Dimensions**: Cylindrical chambers **6 meters long by 1 meter in diameter**, with a useful volume of **4.7 m³** per chamber.
- **Dual Chambers**:
    - **Chamber A (Treatment)**: Lined with stainless steel for resistance to saturated pyroligneous extract corrosion.
    - **Chamber B (Drying)**: Focused on advanced thermal insulation (rock wool/ceramic fiber) for primary drying.
- **Immersion Method**: Refinement from high pressure to **saturated immersion of Pyroligneous Extract (PE)** at atmospheric pressure, increasing operational safety and lowering CAPEX costs.

**ABNT NBR ISO 16852 (Flame Arresters)**
Mandatory installation in the non-condensable gas line, with the function of cooling flame fronts and mitigating the risk of flashback to the primary reactor.

---

### Axis IV — Thermodynamics and Thermal Cascading

The project's sophistication is based on the strict separation between two flow universes:

- **Dirty Universe:** pyrolysis gases and smoke (organic volatiles, CO, H₂, methane)
- **Clean Universe:** hot air from the outer jacket and plenum

The system employs an insulated *Rocket Stove* burner to consume residual gases (CO, H₂, methane), generating heat for the boiler. The natural draft of the vertical tube (riser) creates neutral pressure (0 barg) in the carbonization chamber, eliminating the need for mechanical exhaust fans in contact with smoke.

| Circuit | Source | Function and Destination |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Flow 1 — Gases/Smoke** | Reactor interior (volatiles) | PE production via tubular condenser |
| **Flow 2 — Clean Hot Air** | Outer jacket / plenum | Primary bamboo drying at 60–80 °C |
| **Flow 3 — Chemical Steam** | NR-13 Boiler | Water (80%) + PE (20%) → steam with phenols for cooking and structural bamboo preservation |

---

### Axis V — Environmental Compliance and Economic Viability

Licensing meets the emission limits established by **CONAMA Resolution No. 382/2006** for stationary sources.

Economically, the viability of the Base Module is anchored in the **VERRA VM0044** methodology, which quantifies CO₂ removal through long-term carbon sequestration in the soil via bamboo biochar. Monitoring and verification (MRV) will be supported by the **Automated Geospatial Monitoring System (AGMS/SMGA)**, detailed in the [Technical Note: SMGA](./TAK_nota-tecnica-smga.md), which integrates satellite data and taxonomic occurrences to ensure scientific traceability of biomass stocks.

## 3. Annexes: Development and Engineering Memorial (R&D)

### Annex A — Comprehensive Risk Matrix

#### Table A.1: Technical and Thermodynamic Risks

| Risk Identified | Probability | Impact | Mitigation Strategy |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Creep and Structural Warping** | Medium | High | Carbon steel sheets (min. 3 mm) with internal lining of high-alumina refractory bricks for thermal inertia |
| **Volumetric Explosion (Flashback)** | Low | Critical | Certified flame arrester ABNT NBR ISO 16852, operating by diameter extinction |
| **Condenser Blockage (Siphon)** | High | Medium | Strict maintenance of the tubular condenser slope (10° to 30° relative to the ground) |
| **Pressure Vessel Failure** | Low | Critical | Full compliance with NR-13; relief valves tested at MAWP; water level indicators |

#### Table A.2: Operational, Logistical, and OPEX Risks

| Logistical / OPEX Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation Strategy |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Drop in Biochar Yield** | Medium | Medium | Fluctuations due to Amazonian climatic humidity will be absorbed by focusing the initial business model on VERRA credits (guaranteed sale) and high-value-added structural bamboo |
| **Fossil Diesel Price Shock** | High | High | *Logistical Sovereignty:* Phase 2 will create the *Alcanol-Mix* (ethanol/methanol) to supply the internal fleet, shielding the project against predatory freight rates in the North of the country |
| **E2G Enzyme Price Volatility** | High | High | Financial stress modeling (break-even). As the E2G module is inserted only in Phase 2, if inputs make OPEX unviable, the system reverts to the consolidated stability of the Base Module (Layer 1) |

---

### Annex B — Development Roadmap TRL 4 to TRL 7

Scaling requires a mandatory digital simulation stage before committing physical CAPEX. Evolution is structured over 18 months:

**Phase 1 — Detail Engineering and Design (Months 1–3)**
Finalization of mass and energy flow diagrams, mechanical calculations (NR-13), and automation project using frequency inverters.

**Phase 1.5 — Digital Twin and Computational Validation (Month 4)**
Use of industrial simulation (Aspen Plus or DWSIM) to model the energy balance. The simulation will mathematically demonstrate that leveraging residual heat does not compromise the thermal energy required to maintain the natural draft of the *Rocket Stove* burner.

**Phase 2 — Fabrication and Assembly — Base Module (Months 5–9)**
Machining of the 3 mm steel reactor, refractory lining application, *Rocket Stove* welding, and NR-13 boiler integration.

**Phase 3 — Commissioning and Cold Tests (Months 10–11)**
Leak tests, Type-K thermocouple calibration, and ambient air flow simulation.

**Phase 4 — Base Module Operation and E2G/CCU Validation (Months 12–18)**
First biochar batches, carbon removal certification (VERRA VM0044). Practical tests of 20% pyroligneous extract infusion into the boiler steam bath. Initiation of pilot integration of Synthetic Methanol routes.

---

### Annex C — Integration of Hybrid Fuels and Methanol Economy Module (Phase 2)

After Layer 1 validation, redundant heat from the plenum will serve as a platform for breaking down lignin from leaf waste into fermentable cellulose, enabling 2nd Generation Ethanol (E2G) production. Biogenic CO₂ released during fermentation, combined with reactor vapors, will be channeled for catalytic hydrogenation:

$$CO_2 + 3H_2 \rightleftharpoons CH_3OH + H_2O$$

This process converts CO₂ into storable liquid methanol — the foundation of the **Methanol Economy**, a concept validated globally by Prof. George A. Olah (1994 Nobel Laureate in Chemistry). The strategy transforms biogenic emissions into liquid fuel, shielding the project from fossil fuel dependency in the Amazonian logistical context.

### Annex D — Geospatial Monitoring System and Open Data Platform

**Status:** Proposed | **Integration:** Axis II — Ecological Management and MRV Component

#### D.1 Rationale and Strategic Alignment

Quantifying bamboo biomass potential in Amazonian territories is a necessary condition for three central pillars of the Platform: (i) sizing the sustainable supply of *Guadua* spp. for the biorefinery; (ii) certifying carbon removal via biochar using the VERRA VM0044 methodology; and (iii) technical defense of the project before national funders (BNDES, Amazon Fund, Climate Fund) and the international scientific community in the COP30 context.

The Southwestern Amazon concentrates an estimated 4.5 to 7 million hectares dominated by *Guadua weberbaueri* and *G. sarcocarpa* in Acre alone — making it the largest native bamboo forest in the world. Despite this magnitude, systematic and publicly accessible geospatial inventories remain scarce.

This Annex describes the technical architecture of the **Automated Geospatial Monitoring System (AGMS)**, built on free and open-source tools, capable of producing auditable, versioned, and citable data for use in project defense, scientific publications, and institutional transparency platforms.

---

#### D.2 System Architecture — Three Integrated Layers

The AGMS operates on three overlapping and complementary layers, integrated into a centralized GitHub repository and automatically published as a public portal via MkDocs.

##### D.2.1 Layer 1 — Taxonomic Occurrences

**Goal:** map all georeferenced records of the four priority genera (*Guadua*, *Phyllostachys*, *Bambusa*, *Dendrocalamus*) in Brazil, with historical series since the 19th century.

| Source | Coverage | Technical Access | Relevance |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **GBIF** | Global (~2 billion records) | REST API via `pygbif` | Distribution of the 4 genera in Brazil with coordinates, dates, and collector |
| **speciesLink (CRIA)** | Brazilian herbaria | REST API specieslink.net | Acrean herbarium records, including UFAC collections |
| **iNaturalist** | Community observations with photo | Passed to GBIF (Research-Grade) | Participatory validation and passive field monitoring |
| **Flora and Funga of Brazil (REFLORA)** | National official taxonomy | IBGE Portal + API | Synonymy and conservation status by species |

**Automated collection script structure:**

```python
from pygbif import occurrences as occ
import pandas as pd

genera = ["Guadua", "Phyllostachys", "Bambusa", "Dendrocalamus"]
for genus in genera:
    result = occ.search(
        genus=genus,
        country="BR",
        hasCoordinate=True,
        hasGeospatialIssue=False,
        limit=5000
    )
    df = pd.DataFrame(result["results"])
    df.to_csv(f"data/occurrences/{genus}_BR.csv", index=False)
```

**Product:** four CSV files per genus, with latitude, longitude, collection date, municipality, state, collector, and source. Automatically updated via GitHub Actions every Monday.

##### D.2.2 Layer 2 — Historical Satellite Series

**Goal:** extract time series of vegetation indices and biomass estimates over the mapped occurrence areas in Layer 1, with historical coverage starting in 1985 (Landsat 5 TM).

**Main satellite sources:**

| Platform | Resolution | Period | Variable |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Landsat 5/7/8/9 (USGS) | 30 m | 1985–present | NDVI, NBR, land cover |
| Sentinel-2 (ESA) | 10 m | 2017–present | High-resolution NDVI, species detection |
| GEDI (NASA/ISS) | 25 m | 2019–present | Canopy height, aboveground biomass |
| PRODES/DETER (INPE) | 20–30 m | 1988–present | Deforestation and forest degradation |

##### D.2.3 Layer 3 — Integration, Transparency, and Open Science

All AGMS code, data, and products are published in a public repository under a CC BY 4.0 license, with a permanent DOI via Zenodo for each consolidated version. Any researcher can open an issue or pull request to contribute field data, refine classifications, or validate estimates — converting the AGMS into a **collaborative public good** aligned with the open science principles required by the Amazon Fund and Horizon Europe.
