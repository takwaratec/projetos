### The Bamboo Market in Brazil

#### What is the R&D scenario? Is there bamboo to meet the demands?

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*SA5rC1zx8eZlEj7M6jBqSw.jpeg)

---

Brazil is known as the country of biodiversity, and for good reason: its territory corresponds to 17% of the tropical land area, 12% of surface waters, and 11% of all known plants on the planet. And of course, we have bamboo.

Bamboo has around 1,600 species from 120 genera; 40% of the species are in the Americas. Brazil is home to 35 genera and 258 native species, 120 of which are endemic—they only occur here! The state of Acre alone contains the largest natural bamboo forest in the world, 4.5 million hectares forming a 150,000 km² patch that extends to the Andes, passing through the state of Amazonas, Colombia, and Peru.

A collection of scientific articles published in Brazilian universities (EMBRAPA, 2019 "The Brazilian Bamboo"), although more than 90% of their content deals with Asian species and despite the unfavorable public policies, one can notice the interest in developing this production chain in the country—the question remains for whom.

R&D investments are mediocre; bamboo is still considered an agricultural pest in the country. It does not even appear as a transversal theme in the curricula of natural and agricultural sciences, nor in any of the engineerings, whether as a subject or a noble material. There are no specialized technical or professional training schools for bamboo in the public system.

On September 8, 2011, Law 12,484 was enacted, which instituted the National Policy to Encourage Sustainable Management and Cultivation of Bamboo (PNMCB), aimed at developing bamboo culture in Brazil through government actions and private ventures. Article 3 reads:

"The guidelines of the PNMCB are the development of centers for sustainable management, cultivation, and processing of bamboo, especially in regions with the highest occurrence of natural stocks of the plant, in regions where agricultural production is based on family production units, and around centers creating technologies applicable to the product."

While public policies are not effectively adopted, the market adjusts with its own rules. Some questionable achievements were celebrated by enthusiasts and investors, such as Brazil's entry into INBAR (International Network for Bamboo And Rattan), an organization created in China to support bamboo development worldwide—an encourager of commodities with more than 40 member countries. The technical cooperation agreements sealed with China so far have resulted in the aforementioned publication, technical visits between the two countries, and the development of research focused on cultivating Asian species in national territory, some previously non-existent on Brazilian soil. What is not yet seen are guidelines focused on natural stocks and the highly desired share of social participation in the sustainable development proclaimed with bamboo.

It would be unfair not to mention the state of Acre, which promoted significant advances in legislation, gaining "permission" for bamboo extractivism in the Amazon rainforest, which should have long been included in the list of timber forest products, avoiding the illegal felling of thousands of trees. The state of Santa Catarina is also an example of legislative progress, and numerous initiatives from associations, study groups, and promotion efforts initiated by society without any government oversight cause a new sustainable business focused on bamboo to emerge exponentially in the country every day.

Despite not yet appearing in numbers in the financial sector, bamboo has been used in Brazil since ancient times. It is possible to find artifacts from that time in the Imperial Museum, along with numerous publications with illustrations and reports of weaving used to this day in mats for building linings, ox carts, sieves, baskets, furniture, fences, and various utensils, not to mention the production of indigenous peoples. In academia, it is possible to find articles from the 1970s dealing with its use in irrigation as pipes and gutters for conducting water and in the construction of constructive structures.

In the state of Pernambuco, until a few years ago, a company from the Tapajós group operated since the 80s in the manufacture of cardboard with cellulose extracted from the largest commercial artificial bamboo forest in the Americas. According to information given to the press, the company would migrate to the field of activated bamboo charcoal, but no signal has yet been given.

Except for the Caatinga, bamboo is found naturally in all Brazilian biomes, often configuring a serious environmental problem due to the invasive behavior of some species, especially sprawling Asian exotics that develop aggressively, suffocating native environments.

It is believed, for lack of in-depth study, that in addition to commercial plantations stimulated by the Law, added to the large Amazon patch, there may still be hundreds of thousands of hectares of bamboo scattered throughout the country, especially in the Atlantic Forest, without any type of management, study, or use.

Having current legislation as an obstacle, which prohibits cutting bamboo under the argument of environmental preservation, what remains is a gigantic waste of raw material and the release of tons of non-fixed carbon due to spontaneous degradation, delaying the creation of thousands of jobs and income for vulnerable populations kept away from the consumer society behind the forests of protected bamboo.

Even without a defined management policy, Brazil has already managed to approve building standard NBR16828-1 in 12/2020, signaling with a horizon of enthusiasm for investment in social interest housing and low environmental impact.

As can be seen, concerning bamboo, the market in Brazil is an ocean of opportunities.
