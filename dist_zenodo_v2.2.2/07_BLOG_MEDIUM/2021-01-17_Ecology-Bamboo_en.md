### THE ECOLOGY OF BAMBOO

#### The systems thinking taught in the ancient East by the 150-million-year-old plant that is today an archetype of humanity.

![2019-Fabio Takwara](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*NTR_LCC9mCV4MvoU9cw-OA.jpeg)

Phyllostachys Edulis Forest in the Águas Emendadas Ecological Reserve-DF

Bamboo is one of the oldest plants on the planet and considered the biomaterial most used by humanity. Paleontologists claim that bamboo was the primary diet of dinosaurs in the Cretaceous period more than 100 million years ago. In 2019, [scientists from India](https://www.thehindu.com/sci-tech/science/india-is-home-to-asias-oldest-bamboo/article29605048.ece) obtained fossil evidence of two new bamboo species dating back to the Oligocene period (25 million years). This discovery places India as the birthplace of bamboo, rather than China, as previously believed.

To understand the importance of this discovery, imagine that bamboo has been improving its genetic structure throughout its existence to resist drastic climate changes and significant catastrophes faced by the planet. Furthermore, it was a witness to the emergence and a tool for the development of humanity.

We can say that this plant was fundamental to human development, as it served as food and shelter for thousands of years before the evolution of agriculture and the introduction of other species. Storage instruments, artifacts, vessels, various fuels, and gears of many different types were also made of bamboo. Fossils of bamboo utensils are rare in archaeological studies, precisely because of its renewable nature. However, its appearance in contact with humans can be dated to 400,000 years ago as a friction artifact to produce fire, and 3,000 years ago in China as the Book of Changes, the "I Ching"—formed of bundles of slats tied at the ends, since paper had not yet been invented. Although widely used, bamboo constructions were not preserved for long periods, but it is known that the Taj Mahal in India, built in 1653, has its dome made of bamboo, perhaps being the oldest structural work ever identified. Rural constructions over 100 years old can be found in the provinces of Japan, Vietnam, China, India, and Indonesia.

Bamboo is known as the largest CO2 sequesterer from the atmosphere in the entire plant kingdom, and because it has a short life cycle, varying between 10 and 20 years, it needs to be used in all its life phases, thus providing the appropriate management for the best use of the crop. Old individuals consume the energy needed for the development of younger ones, and the absence of light causes growth to be interrupted, causing deformations and useless raw material for manufacturing.

Being a characteristic of plants, as well as all living beings, that when they die they release all the stored carbon back into the atmosphere, it is necessary to fix this carbon by harvesting the mature individuals and transforming them into utensils with a longer life. But, being an organic material, its decomposition is also fast, and this would have been perhaps humanity's greatest challenge: to preserve bamboo works for the longest possible life.

To get an idea of the magnificent interaction of bamboo with the planet's self-management mechanism, it was the only plant species that sprouted spontaneously after the 1945 environmental catastrophe caused by the atomic bombs dropped by the US on Hiroshima and Nagasaki in Japan. In other words, bamboo signaled that from that moment on, there would be conditions for the regeneration of life in that place.

The [reductionist-mechanistic thought](https://www.webartigos.com/artigos/o-pensamento-reducionista/5774/) that separates parts from the whole and was inherited from philosophers of the 17th-century Scientific Revolution, such as Descartes and Newton, interfered with the [systemic development](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pensamento_sist%C3%AAmico) process that is intrinsic to nature and all life models on the planet. Just as the human body is made up of organs that interact with each other to maintain life, so are plants and other beings that all interact with each other and maintain the harmonious flow of our existence. Recent centuries have been marked by numerous demonstrations of the exhaustion of natural resources and human life itself due to this thought, and especially in the last century, bamboo has been presented to Western peoples already inserted into this predatory form of use. This breaks with a tradition of ancient peoples, who attributed to it a primordial philosophical function.

In the East, Seven Lessons of Bamboo are taught:

1. Bamboo resists the storm because it had the greatness and humility to bow before it, in respect for its Divine nature;
2. Bamboo has deep roots, for only so is it possible to grow and reach heights, demonstrating respect for ancestry;
3. Bamboo lives in community, and each individual is responsible for the existence of the other; that is, cooperation leads to success;
4. Bamboo's goal is the sky, and therefore it does not create branches that could hinder its climb and that of its brothers. It does not cling to futilities;
5. Bamboo has no ego—it collects in its nodes all the stones and thorns of the path, accepting its imperfection and gaining support to rise with the gifts received, in community;
6. Bamboo is hollow, it is empty of itself, it is welcoming, always being available to receive what comes from outside, without resentment;
7. Bamboo lives in search of the things of the Most High, of PEACE and the common GOOD.

Bamboo, therefore, exists as if it had no reason to be and thus teaches us to supply all our basic needs collaboratively, so that in community each individual can support and allow the existence of the other in their best performance.

That reductionist thought adopted in recent centuries synthesized and distorted in a repetitive and mechanical way all the efforts that Nature and the human being, with all their genius, built throughout their existences in an integrated way. With the industrial revolution, the human being became a reproductive machine and used nature as raw material for all the artifacts of mass death.

In the 20th century, numerous brilliant minds noticed this destructive fragmentation coming from the evolution of civilizations and produced several works for the reconstruction of systems thinking—which was natural to original peoples—bringing it as one of the keys to the solution of the most serious problems affecting all societies in the modern world. Here are some examples:

* The celebrated American scientist [Carl Sagan](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Sagan), with the TV series Cosmos, unveils many of the mysteries of the universe for humanity. Among his more than 600 books, in "The Demon-Haunted World," he questions the bases that guide modern societies in the light of science.
* The Austrian physicist [Fritjof Capra](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritjof_Capra), in his first commercial book, "The TAO of Physics," presents science from a philosophical point of view, going against all the concepts that governed it until then. He became, as a writer, the greatest decoder of systems thinking, creating theories applicable to the resolution of humanity's economic and social problems under the scientific gaze. He inspired theories of network connections and the appropriation and application of scientific knowledge by the individuals themselves for the benefit of their communities.
* The Polish sociologist [Zygmunt Bauman](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zygmunt_Bauman) and his Liquid Modernity refer to the volatile state of consciousness of postmodernity and expose in a clear and limpid way how consumer societies, with their technical social media tools, spread a gigantic amount of information, but because of individualistic practices, do nothing to help in the application of knowledge.
* Influenced by the complexity of the term "[Ecology](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecologia)" (the word deriving from the Greek junction *Ökologie*, eco (*Oikos*) House and logia (*Logos*) Study), created by the German scientist [Ernst Haeckel](http://Ernest%20Haeckel) in 1866, the French philosopher [Edgar Morin](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edgar_Morin) in 1977 published his great work "The Method," creating [complex thinking](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complexidade#:~:text=Frequentemente%20%C3%A9%20tamb%C3%A9m%20chamada%20teoria,da%20complexidade%20ou%20pensamento%20complexo.) where he grounds the Seven Pillars for the Education of the Future, of which I make a free interpretation and an analogy with the 7 lessons of bamboo here:

1. Starting from own life experience, receiving the forces of nature with humility, respect, and interaction, bowing and living the path;
2. Filtering the sources of information, orienting by solid bases and creating roots;
3. Understanding the three-dimensional reality of the human condition, which interacts with other beings around it, and developing the community spirit;
4. Establishing empathy with the other, understanding and accepting the human condition and not creating branches that hinder mutual development;
5. Knowledge is changeable, actions generate conflicts and uncertainties, learning from mistakes and creating support nodes, dissolving the ego and winning allies;
6. Accepting the complexity of the phenomena of time and space, remaining hollow and empty of certainties;
7. Adopting ethics as the limit of action, respecting own condition as a three-dimensional, biological, individual, and social being.

This evident sensation of coincidence that reading the Seven Pillars produces when compared to the Seven Lessons of Bamboo is explained by the concept of [**Archetype**](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arqu%C3%A9tipo), which was created by another great influencer of this blessed century: the Swiss psychiatrist [Carl Jung](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Jung). This concept translates as an ancient model to be followed; it's as if something represented a prototype or referred to old impressions about something. In this sense, I dare to declare that bamboo is an archetype of humanity.

The 21st century opens its doors under the light of these regenerating influences of the human condition on Earth. From the practical and reality-transforming point of view, the term [Permaculture](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permacultura#:~:text=Permacultura%20%C3%A9%20a%20utiliza%C3%A7%C3%A3o%20de,sentido%20de%20um%20futuro%20sustent%C3%A1vel.), proposed by the Australian researcher and natural scientist [Bill Mollison](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Mollison) in the early 1970s, represents an instruction manual on how to contemplate these principles with practical actions in the community sphere. Permaculture is defined as a holistic method for planning, updating, and maintaining environmentally sustainable, socially just, and financially viable human-scale systems. Such is the complexity of this definition that humanity will still take a few decades for the precise application of the term. Strongly influenced by the ideas of the Japanese philosopher and farmer [Masanobu Fukuoka](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masanobu_Fukuoka), author of the book "The One-Straw Revolution," which presents the Fukuoka Method of ecological agriculture, Permaculture—which means permanent culture—went far beyond agriculture. It brought together a diversity of knowledge and scientific teachings and popular wisdom on how to act balanced in communities and has influenced several generations of today's thinkers.

It could be said that Permaculture is a system for harmonizing realities. It is an instrument that allows gathering relevant information about a situation to be faced and finding the most harmonious solution to be applied within the principles and concepts of Ecology, which in turn are synthesized in the Seven Pillars of Modern Education and which were taught for millennia in the Seven Lessons of Bamboo.
