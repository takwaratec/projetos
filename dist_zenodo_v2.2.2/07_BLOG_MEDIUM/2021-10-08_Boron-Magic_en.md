### Boron Magic

Rare metalloid in the Solar System, it corresponds to 0.001% of the Earth's Crust.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*I0oHxxPtvhaSq5v6xaW2Ng.jpeg)

Boron is an alien: cosmic rays that exploded against the Earth, precipitating into ultra-resistant crystals when in contact with hydrated salt, Borax, **Sodium Borate**, or **Sodium Tetraborate**. It is economically exploited as a mineral in more than 150 compounds that vary according to geology due to oxidations, calcium and magnesium hydrates, and volcanic activity.

Possibly the force of this impact of light is responsible for plant and even human life on Earth, as Boron is an essential element for plants and is associated with the seven Ultra-trace Elements—a group of minerals present in all organisms that perform metabolic functions unknown to this day. They are Arsenic, Boron, Fluorine, Germanium, Lithium, Silicon, and Vanadium. The concentration of these elements is extremely small in organisms, and external administration as a supplement, medicine, or insecticide must be strictly monitored.

The largest reserve of Borax is in Turkey (63%), almost untapped, and the largest commercial open-pit mine is in the USA, in the Mojave Desert in California, responsible for 50% of the world's production of Borates. These are used in aerospace research for the construction of spacecraft, as fiberglass, ceramic compounds, agricultural inputs, medicines, and insecticides. Its use has been known since Babylon, and currently, world consumption has reached such high levels that the planet's Borax reserves—including those of Turkey, Chile, China, Bolivia, Germany, Peru, Russia, and Argentina—may be exhausted in less than 200 years. The trivialization of its use is so alarming that it can be found in high concentrations everywhere: in rivers, effluents, and urban dumps due to widespread use in agriculture or in the form of detergents and pesticides, promoting the death of countless species of fish and arthropods such as beetles, butterflies, centipedes, and contaminating groundwater through inadequate disposal. Research indicates that regular contact can affect the male reproductive system of humans, also causing skin irritation and respiratory problems when inhaled.

At this rate, either the Borax will run out or it will end us, and not even the environment will remain.
