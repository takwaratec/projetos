### Bamboo Without Management: The Urgency of Regulation and the Risks of False Carbon Credits

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*4ahc64zDRDryspmIhpMsAQ.png)

While dying, bamboo releases all sequestered carbon into the atmosphere.

The growing interest in carbon credits as a climate mitigation mechanism has drawn attention to various production chains, including bamboo. However, the absence of specific protocols and clear legal devices creates a scenario conducive to misleading—and possibly criminal—promises regarding carbon capture and storage in these plantations. This article analyzes the risks of bamboo without proper management, exposes Brazilian regulatory gaps, and proposes concrete ways to address this problem.

#### The Bamboo Paradox: Potential and Danger

Brazil is home to the greatest diversity of bamboos in the Americas, with 200 to 300 native species, and the state of Acre possesses the largest native bamboo forest in the world, dominated by species of the genus *Guadua*. Despite this natural wealth and a Federal Law (12,484/2011) that established the National Policy to Encourage Sustainable Management and Cultivation of Bamboo, the sector remains underdeveloped and in need of complementary regulation.

### Unmanaged Bamboo: A Source of Emissions

Scientific research demonstrates that unmanaged bamboo can become a significant environmental problem. Studies from the Federal University of Acre, led by Prof. Dra. Sonaira Souza da Silva, reveal that:

* Forest fires dramatically accelerate the expansion of *Guadua* genus bamboos in the Southwestern Amazon.
* Burned areas present 7 to 9 times more bamboo culms per hectare than preserved forests (about 5,000 culms/ha vs. 600 culms/ha).
* Burned forests lose 50% to 74% of their trees, with bamboo suppressing the natural regeneration of tree species.
* Bamboo dominance reduces above-ground biomass to just 51–73% of unburned forest levels, severely compromising carbon sequestration.

When a bamboo culm reaches maturity (3–7 years) and is not harvested, it enters senescence and decomposes in 7–10 years, releasing back into the atmosphere most of the carbon it had sequestered. This spontaneous decomposition represents a significant "carbon waste," transforming what could be a permanent sink into a temporary source.

#### The Fire-Bamboo Cycle: A Dangerous Feedback

The life cycle of *Guadua* species is particularly problematic. They flower, fruit, and die synchronously every 28–30 years, depositing enormous amounts of dry necromass on the soil—a perfect fuel for fires. Human activity (deforestation, agricultural burning) introduces frequent ignition sources into this naturally flammable system, creating a feedback loop:

This cycle results in the conversion of 120,000 hectares of open forest into bamboo-dominated forest—a degraded, homogenized, and low-biodiversity ecosystem.

#### Carbon Credit Fraud in the Voluntary Market

The global voluntary carbon market has been the target of growing greenwashing scandals. In Brazil, the Federal Police's "Operation Greenwashing" (June 2024) dismantled a criminal organization that sold R$ 180 million in carbon credits generated from land-grabbed (griladas) public lands in the Amazon.

#### Identified Systemic Problems

The investigations revealed:

* Grabbing of 538,000 hectares of public lands through duplication and falsification of titles.
* Use of fraudulent Rural Environmental Registries (CAR) to certify projects.
* Issuance of fake environmental licenses with the participation of public servants.
* Illegal extraction of over 1 million m³ of wood, causing an estimated environmental damage of R$ 606 million.
* Creation of "ghost" cattle to meet environmental restrictions.

National and foreign companies bought these credits, including multinationals like Gol, Nestlé, PwC, Toshiba, Spotify, and Boeing. Even internationally recognized certifiers, such as Verra, suspended projects following the allegations, highlighting the fragility of verification mechanisms.

#### Bamboo in the Carbon Market: Critical Gaps

Bamboo faces particular obstacles in generating legitimate credits:

1. **Absence of Certified Methodology:** There is no widely accepted methodology by Verra or Gold Standard specifically designed for bamboo. The few existing projects adapt A/R (Afforestation/Reforestation) methodologies created for tree forests, which do not adequately capture bamboo's unique dynamics (annual harvest, short life cycle, selective management).
2. **Botanical Classification:** As a grass, bamboo faces barriers for inclusion in mechanisms like REDD+, which focus on "trees."
3. **Lack of Traceability:** The absence of robust origin certification and traceability systems makes it difficult to verify the legitimacy of credits, opening space for fraud.
4. **Questionable Additionality:** Without clear protocols, it is impossible to verify if the sequestered carbon is additional (i.e., if it would not occur anyway) or if it only represents the maintenance of existing stocks.

#### Chemical Treatment of Bamboo: The Hidden Side of Toxicity

The bamboo production chain carries another environmental liability rarely discussed: the toxicity of chemical treatments. A recent study published in the *Brazilian Journal of Science* on the artisanal production of *Guadua angustifolia* in Acre revealed alarming data.

#### Impacts of Carcinogenic Toxicity

Human Carcinogenic Toxicity represented 93% of the total environmental impact of artisanal bamboo pole production. This toxicity is directly linked to the use of boric acid and borax in chemical treatment, with:

* 91% of the impact related to generated waste (shavings, cuts, discarded poles—13% of total production).
* Effluents discharged into the soil representing 10% of the water used, threatening local water resources.
* Direct risks to the health of workers exposed to the chemicals.

Other relevant impact categories include:

* Freshwater Eutrophication (4%) due to the use of nitrogen and phosphate fertilizers in planting.
* Water Use (1.6%), despite high Amazonian rainfall.

#### Safer Alternatives Ignored

Despite the evidenced problems, less harmful alternative techniques—such as thermal treatments or nanotechnology applications—remain underutilized in Brazil. Brazilian research has already validated the effectiveness of pyroligneous acid (a carbonization byproduct) as a natural bamboo treatment agent, but large-scale adoption faces a lack of government guidelines and incentives.

Conventional chemicals used in bamboo—such as CCB (chromated copper borate) and CCA (chromated copper arsenate)—are toxic and can contaminate soil and water when treated products are discarded. Law 12,484/2011 mentions the development of management technologies and applications but has not regulated aspects of chemical safety or waste disposal.

#### Regulation: The Gap Between Law and Practice

Federal Law No. 12,484/2011 represents an important legal framework but remains largely inoperative due to the absence of complementary regulations.

#### What the Law Provides (and Has Not Been Implemented)

The National Policy to Encourage Sustainable Management and Cultivation of Bamboo establishes clear guidelines, including:

* Valuation of bamboo as an agro-silvo-cultural product for ecological, economic, and social needs.
* Incentives for technological development.
* Guarantee of favored rural credit for family farmers.
* Technical assistance and certification of origin and quality.
* Establishment of partnerships and stimulation of trade.

However, more than a decade after its enactment, the proposed instruments have not materialized. Ministries (Agriculture, Finance) and development banks (BNDES) have not created programs, allocated budgets, or defined rules for accessing incentives.

#### Legal Insecurity and Cultural Prejudice

The lack of regulation creates an environment of deep legal insecurity:

* Environmental licensing criteria vary between states.
* The Forest Code (Law 12,651/2012) allows the collection of non-timber forest products, but interventions in APPs or cutting of native species require specific authorizations from state agencies, with inconsistent criteria.
* Cultural and institutional prejudice against bamboo persists, with species being erroneously classified as "pests" or "invasive," even when they are native or clumping (non-invasive).

This regulatory vacuum is the biggest deterrent to long-term investment. Entrepreneurs hesitate to allocate capital to plantations or processing plants when rules are not clear, stable, or uniform.

#### Ways to Address This: Citizen Science and Social Mobilization

In the face of structural State apathy and growing fraud risks, organized civil society emerges as a protagonist in defending ethical bamboo bioeconomy.

#### Role of Associations and BAmbuBR

BAmbuBR (Brazilian Bamboo Association) and other entities have a crucial role in:

* Promoting research by generating replicable models based on participatory and integrative feasibility studies.
* Disclosing information without reservations, including problematic aspects like the chemical toxicity of treatments—transparency that is essential for a truly sustainable bioeconomy.
* Developing their own self-management projects, ensuring independence from economic interests that could compromise the sector's ethics.

#### Strategic Recommendations Based on Science

To overcome the paradox between "ecological threat" and "economic promise," policies must be led by ecology:

1. **Prioritize Already Degraded Lands:** Economic exploration should primarily focus on the 120,000 hectares already degraded and dominated by bamboo due to fires. This transforms management into a remediation tool, not further degradation.
2. **Develop Low-Impact Harvesting:** Techniques that minimize soil disturbance and canopy opening, considering bamboo's aggressive response to clearings.
3. **Implement Ecological-Economic Zoning:** High-resolution maps (based on research like Prof. Silva's) must define areas for strict conservation vs. areas for controlled exploration.
4. **Integrate Fire Management:** Any exploration plan must be coupled with fire prevention and management in the surrounding landscape.
5. **Foster Interdisciplinary Research:** Cost-benefit analyses that account for lost ecosystem services (carbon, biodiversity) vs. economic gains from products.

#### Proposed Certification Protocol

For legitimate bamboo carbon credits, it is essential to have:

* **Specific Methodology:** Brazilian leadership (Embrapa, UFAC, in partnership with Verra) to create a protocol adapted to the unique cycle of tropical bamboo.
* **Full Traceability:** A blockchain-like system to register origin, managed area, harvested volume, and fixed carbon in products or biochar.
* **Proven Additionality:** Demonstration that the project removes CO₂ beyond the baseline scenario (unmanaged bamboo releasing carbon via decomposition).
* **Permanence:** Guarantees of long-term storage, especially for biochar (carbon fixed for centuries).
* **Social Co-benefits:** Income generation for local communities, environmental education, and low-footprint technology.

#### From Citizen Science to the Defense of National Sovereignty

The issue of bamboo in Brazil transcends a technical debate about bioeconomy. It reveals structural gaps in the State's capacity to translate scientific knowledge into effective public policy and to protect its natural heritage against misappropriation and degradation.

Operation Greenwashing exposed the tip of an iceberg: a voluntary carbon market lacking integrity, where false promises are marketed to global investors and companies, while public lands are grabbed and forests are degraded. In the context of bamboo, the absence of specific protocols and certified methodologies makes the sector especially vulnerable to these frauds.

Critical and investigative journalism, combined with citizen science and social mobilization, becomes essential for democracy and national sovereignty. This article, the result of an independent investigation based on official sources and academic research, seeks to alert those who delude themselves with easy promises of carbon credits and call society to action.

The future of Brazilian bamboo—whether it will be a tool for regeneration or a vector for degradation—depends on our ability to face this reality with courage, transparency, and scientific commitment.
