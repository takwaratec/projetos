### Carbon x Poverty

#### Understand how the carbon to be fixed in Brazil can eradicate hunger and misery, perhaps even save humanity.

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*obossnhu3uaHMYQzxLSYkQ.jpeg)

Poverty is undoubtedly the biggest problem to be faced in our country. Far from thinking it is possible to resolve all the ills exposed here with a single proposal, I vehemently believe that the outline presented here can serve as a basis for numerous social, corporate, and governmental actions on a global scale.

Climate changes are at the top of the priorities of all the world's nations, and the promised catastrophic effects, should the agreements not be met, will not distinguish between poor and rich; in fact, humanity has been reaping the misfortunes of these alterations since the First World War, when greed took control and the planet transformed into a stage for mass destruction.

Notwithstanding the effects that were already being produced from the 19th century with the Industrial Revolution, the war industry equipped numerous corporations with technology never imagined. All the sciences applied for the killing of human beings in combat were destined for the exhaustion of the planet's natural resources.

Only in 1972 was the alarm given with the first World Conference on the Environment, the Stockholm Conference, which was the milestone of the notion of Sustainable Development, where developed countries, which had already concluded their share of destruction and spread their tentacles around the world, defended the reduction of industrialization of developing countries, creating a dispute that was marked by "zero development" vs. "development at any cost."

The last five decades were marked by the dominance of "Economic Power," an omnipresent entity that dictates market rules and decides the dose of technology each nation will have access to for its own development. Infiltrated in all sovereign summits, it influences decision-making in parliaments, universities, and corporations. It defines the sustainability rules of each nation, and the Sustainable Development triad, formed by Social > Ecological > Economic, gives way to "sustainable business," leaving society aside.

Production matrices remain unchanged: ore, wood, oil, and water. Technology is employed in the production of unemployment with the computerization of processes, increasingly powerful machines, and robotics taking the place of humans. Professional qualification in the dispute for vacancies becomes more refined, narrowing the channel of opportunities for the less favored. Remote production gains ground, society moves further and further away from its own reality, and the owners of the ball continue to dictate the rules.

Meanwhile, the "civilized" world protects itself behind norms, seals, and certifications that are demanded of its developing subjects in trade agreements. The segregation imposed by such requirements, which results in arbitrary sanctions, eliminates most of the world's population from the balance, deprived of the technological advancement accessible only to corporations with foreign capital and multinationals, allowing the giants of the corporate world to take advantage of the loopholes in the legislation of developing countries where they plant their facilities, producing on foreign soil what is prohibited in their homeland.

The impacts, losses, and damages caused during the occupation are computed in the performance of the host nation, which classifies it as collateral damage of the progress achieved and "compensated" through tax collection and the jobs generated. This infinite circle is what is putting an end to Earth's resources.

China is a strong example of subordination to this system, closed to the Western world until 1966, when it began a slow cultural revolution that gradually "westernized" some existing customs and processes in the country. Along with economic growth, problems also arose; agreements with developed nations included, in exchange for the transfer of technologies, accepting the industrial waste produced by them, and gradually China became the largest toxic trash can on the planet.

In the 1980s/90s, the capital Beijing came to occupy second place among the most polluted capitals in the world; however, investments in R&D did not cease. China is by far the country that invests the most in Science and Technology; the efforts employed in reducing emissions left Beijing outside the 200 most polluted capitals in 2019, although Linfen and Tianjin still figure as the two most polluted cities in the world. In response, the Chinese government publicly assumed the commitment to zero emissions by 2050.

Recalling Fermi's paradox (Enrico Fermi 1901–1954), winner of the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1938, creator of nuclear fission and who became famous for the phrase "Where is everybody?", uttered in an informal conversation about the existence of other civilizations in the solar system in 1950, it suggests that we are alone, there is no intelligent life beyond the human race in our system, and all efforts to reach other worlds may result in self-extinction by the exhaustion of natural resources employed for this purpose.

"In other words, the resources that alien civilizations have at their disposal are, in order of magnitude, the same for all of them, including us. In this line of thought, if we are an exception in the Universe we have a high probability of collapse or extinction, while if we assume the principle of mediocrity we are led to conclude that very few civilizations are capable of reaching a sufficient technological level to spread into their own solar system before the consumption of their planet's resources triggers a catastrophic population collapse."

This quote is in the article published in Scientific Reports on the [www.nature.com](http://www.nature.com) website on May 6, 2020 (article 7631/2020), which describes a quantitative analysis related to world population growth and deforestation, sentencing the end of the human race in the coming decades, judging by the progressive rhythm identified. The authors classify the Paris Agreement as very weak, due to its strong submission to the economic interests of each country in isolation. And they continue:

"The principle of mediocrity has been questioned, but on the other hand, the idea that humanity is somehow 'special' in the universe has been historically contested several times. Starting with the idea of the Earth as the center of the universe (geocentrism), then the solar system as the center of the universe (heliocentrism), and finally our galaxy as the center of the universe. All these beliefs were denied by facts. Our discussion, focused on resource consumption, shows that whether we assume the principle of mediocrity or our 'uniqueness' as an intelligent species in the universe, the conclusion does not change. Giving a very broad sense to the concept of cultural civilization as a civilization not strongly governed by the economy, we suggest to avoid collapse that only civilizations capable of such a change from an economic society to a kind of 'cultural' society in a timely manner can survive. This discussion leads us to the conclusion that even assuming the principle of mediocrity, the answer to 'Where is everybody?' could be a lugubrious '(almost) everybody is dead'."

What is the recipe? China has been recognized for millennia as the technological reference center for the bamboo production chain in the world. Bamboo, which encompasses 1,300 known species, is a grass of the Poaceae group, like rice, wheat, and oats. It is recognized as the fastest-growing plant, from 15cm to 1m/day, and the largest carbon sequesterer of the plant kingdom, 30x more than eucalyptus per ha/year.

China holds 60% of all known species, is the largest producer of manual and industrialized artifacts, and handles the export and import of bamboo in natura. Data published by INBAR (International Network for Bamboo And Rattan)—an entity created by the Chinese government whose mission is to strengthen the bamboo production chain in other countries, an encourager of commodities—details how transitions occurred in the country's bamboo productive sector.

Until the beginning of its cultural revolution in 1966, China was already a recognized bamboo technological pole. Based on R&D guidelines, research centers were created in strategic provinces to support bamboo producers in the country.

In the 1990s, China began to invest in mass cultivation; until then, the commercial stock was 1 million hectares, including all species. In 2013, the largest artificial bamboo forest for commercial purposes on the planet was formed, composed of a single species (Phyllostachys Edulis) with 6 million hectares, joining 19 provinces and involving approximately 100 million inhabitants in the primary chain.

The bamboo market in the world collects around US$ 60 billion/year. Just with bamboo shoots for food, China employs 10 million people and generates a revenue of US$ 20 billion/year. More than 3,000 uses for bamboo are known, and China dominates them all.

It is undeniable that this productive sector plays a large role in the economy and social development; however, it is impossible not to admit its participation in reducing emissions. Chinese experts claim to be able to fix carbon from 60% of the biomass produced with bamboo in the country, and they still think it's too little, pursuing 100%.
