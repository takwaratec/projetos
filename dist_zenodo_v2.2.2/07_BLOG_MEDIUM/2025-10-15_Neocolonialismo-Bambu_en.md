### Brazilian Bamboo in the Crosshairs of Neocolonialism: How Youth Become Poster Children for Technological Dependency

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*ctffYca_gqcfa6QIfDwCBg.png)

#### An investigation into how China transforms our natural resources into commodities and our youth into ambassadors of a model that perpetuates backwardness.

As the world prepares to converge in Belém in November 2025 for COP30, a silent and meticulous operation is underway. It is not just about commercial agreements or technological partnerships. It is the consolidation of a neocolonial model that transforms Brazil into what we have always been: suppliers of raw materials for industrialized powers, now with the refinement of using our own youth as enthusiastic ambassadors of this dependency.

The case of the bamboo course in China, offered this October 2025 to Brazilians selected through an opaque and politically directed process, is just the tip of the iceberg of a much broader strategy. A strategy that uses the seduction of advanced technology and the political inexperience of a generation to perpetuate one of history's oldest patterns of economic domination: the regressive specialization of peripheral countries.

#### The 5.2 Billion Dollar Green Treasure Being Handed Over on a Silver Platter

Brazil possesses a treasure that few know about and the whole world covets. We are the second-ranking country in bamboo species diversity, with about 300 registered species, mostly concentrated in the Amazon and the Atlantic Forest. In Acre, recent research has revealed an impressive bioeconomic potential: **5.2 billion dollars** just in that state's native bamboo natural reserves.

There are 21.8 billion bamboo stalks spread across forests that occupy 62% of Acre's territory. A volume of more than 800 million cubic meters of renewable biomass that could supply everything from the furniture industry to clean energy production, including civil construction, paper, textiles, and an infinity of high-added-value products.

But there is a crucial detail that enthusiasts of "cooperation" with China prefer not to mention: **Brazil does not need Chinese technology to develop this potential**. What we lack is not technical knowledge—we have universities, research centers, and, above all, **bamboo masters** with decades of practical experience who have mastered sustainable management techniques for generations.

What we lack is **political will** to break with the extractivist model and **resistance to the lobbies** that profit from our condition as a commodity supplier.

#### The Digital Propaganda Machine: Youth as Influencers of Dependency

The choice of young Brazilians for the course in China is neither casual nor benevolent. It is strategic and calculated. China dominates like no other country the techniques of digital marketing and social commerce. With more than 765 million livestreaming users and a live commerce market moving US$ 500 billion annually, the Chinese know exactly how to transform influencers into sales machines.

Brazil has 181 million active social media users. Our youth is hyper-connected to TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, and WhatsApp. Imagine the power of 25 to 30 young Brazilians returning from a "transformative" experience in China, dazzled by the technology presented, creating digital content about the "wonders" of Chinese bamboo products.

It wouldn't be the first time. **Zheng Xiang Xiang**, a Chinese influencer, went worldwide viral by showing products for only 3 seconds in lives, earning R$ 51.6 million in one week. Her technique—repetitive, hypnotic, focused on impulse buying—is already being studied and replicated by Brazilian entrepreneurs.

The difference is that now, instead of generic products, it will be **Chinese bamboo products** being promoted by **young Brazilians** who, in their innocence or naivety, believe they are promoting sustainability and innovation. In reality, they will be promoting our technological dependency and the denationalization of a strategic resource.

#### The Colonial Pattern Repeats: We Export fiber, We Import Manufactured Goods

The model is known and tested. For centuries, Brazil exported brazilwood, sugar, gold, coffee, rubber. Always raw material. Always with the added value being captured by central countries. Today, 80% of our exports to China are commodities: iron ore, soy, oil.

In the bamboo sector, the pattern repeats with surgical precision. China has an internal bamboo market moving US$ 68 billion annually. They dominate all stages of the production chain: from mechanized cultivation to sophisticated products like laminated floors, structural panels, fabrics, high-quality paper, and even bicycles.

What do they want from Brazil? **Raw fiber**. Bamboo cut, dried, and exported in natura to be processed in Chinese factories and return to Brazil as manufactured products sold at prices 10, 20, 50 times higher than the original raw material value.

It is 21st-century colonialism working to perfection: **we provide natural resources, they sell industrialized products**.

But there is a perverse novelty in this model. Besides keeping us as commodity suppliers, they transform our own youth into **enthusiastic promoters** of this dependency. It is cultural capture as a complement to economic capture.

#### The Exclusion of Masters: Eliminating Critical Resistance

One of the most revealing aspects of the selection process for the course in China was the deliberate exclusion of people over 50. Officially justified as a "Chinese requirement," this age barrier had a devastating effect: **excluding exactly the Brazilians who could critically question the model presented**.

Lúcio Ventania and dozens of other bamboo masters, with decades of experience in the sustainable management of native species, were summarily eliminated from the opportunity. People who know in practice the differences between Asian species (domesticated for millennia) and our native species (still wild and with unexplored genetic potential).

People who experienced the struggle for the approval of the Bamboo Law, which has the central objective of **supporting family farmers** and **promoting regional socio-economic development** based on sustainable management.

People who understand that true bamboo bioeconomy in Brazil must be **inclusive, democratic, and nationally sovereign**, not subordinate to the interests of foreign corporations.

The exclusion of these masters was not a bureaucratic accident. It was a strategic necessity. **Youth without political experience** are much more susceptible to technological seduction and less likely to question power asymmetries in international "partnerships."

#### The Parliamentary Front and the Organized Lobby: State Capture

The selection process for the course revealed something even more serious: the complete capture of bamboo public policy by an articulated lobby between the **Bamboo Parliamentary Front** (chaired by deputy Giovani Cherini) and organizations like **APROBAMBU/ABRAFIBRAS**.

Of the 25 to 30 available spots, **15 (60%) were allocated to the Parliamentary Front** for filling by direct indication. There was no selection process, there were no technical criteria, there was no transparency. Just the **distribution of political benefits** using an international opportunity funded by a foreign government.

It is important to contextualize who Giovani Cherini is. The same deputy who, in 2025, is trying to set up a subcommittee in the Chamber to promote the thesis that global warming is "a grand hoax." The same one who plans to take an "agro front" to question climate change at COP30.

This is the politician who controls 60% of the spots for a course on **sustainability** and **bioeconomy**. The irony would be comic if it weren't tragic.

The alliance between this Parliamentary Front and the industrial lobby uses Brazilian State instruments to **strengthen influence networks**, **reward political supporters**, and **promote a technological dependency agenda** that marginalizes exactly the sectors that the Bamboo Law was created to support: **family farmers, traditional communities, and small rural entrepreneurs**.

#### The Grand Chinese Strategy: Beyond Bamboo

The case of bamboo is just a fragment of a much broader strategy. China invested US$ 1.73 billion in Brazil in 2023, a 33% increase. Projections for the coming years exceed R$ 27 billion.

Chinese investments in Brazil concentrate on **strategic sectors**: energy (39%), automotive (33%), mining, logistics, technology, and agribusiness. BYD is investing R$ 5.5 billion in a factory in Bahia, GWM announces R$ 10 billion, and GAC plans R$ 7.4 billion.

It looks like development, **but it is sophisticated dependency**. Chinese factories in Brazil produce for the local market and for regional export, **but the core technology, patents, critical components, and design** continue to be Chinese. We are assemblers, not creators.

In the bamboo sector, the strategy is similar: create a technological dependency that transforms us into **specialized suppliers of raw material** and a **captive consumer market** for Chinese manufactured products.

#### The Green Treasure That Could Set Us Free

The tragedy is that Brazil has **everything** to lead the world's bamboo bioeconomy sovereignly:

**Incomparable biodiversity**: 300 native species, including the spectacular Amazonian **Guadua**, which can reach 30 meters in height and has structural properties superior to many woods.

**Territorial extension**: The Brazilian Amazon possesses the largest natural reserve of bamboo on the planet. In Acre alone, there are 5.2 billion dollars in bioeconomic potential.

**Advanced legal framework**: Law 12,484/2011 establishes a progressive national policy, **focused on social inclusion**, **sustainable management**, and **regional development**. However, it was erased from the bioeconomy policy agenda presented in 2024 by **Marina Silva's MMA**, known for opening the Amazon's doors to industrial groups exploiting riches and ancestral knowledge.

**Traditional knowledge**: Indigenous peoples and traditional communities have managed bamboo for millennia. This knowledge, combined with modern scientific research, could generate **unique innovations in the world**.

**Scientific capacity**: We have universities, research institutes, and qualified professionals to develop our **own technologies** adapted to our biomes and socio-economic realities.

With a sovereign national strategy, Brazil could:

- Develop **Brazilian cultivars** optimized for our soils and climates.
- Create **local production chains** that add value from the base.
- Export **manufactured bamboo products**, not raw fiber.
- Generate **qualified jobs** in rural communities.
- Lead **innovation in tropical bioeconomy**.
- **Replace wood imports**, plastic, and even steel.
- Sequester **millions of tons of carbon** and sell genuinely Brazilian environmental credits.

Instead, we are allowing **youth without critical experience** to be transformed into **promoters of our own dependency**.

#### COP30: The Stage of Contradiction

COP30 in Belém, in November 2025, will be the perfect stage for this contradiction. **Belém inaugurates the world's largest Bioeconomy Park**, with 6,000 square meters dedicated to transforming Amazonian biodiversity into high-added-value businesses.

It's a R$ 300 million project, the fruit of a partnership between the **Pará government and Vale**, symbolizing the region's potential to lead the global green economy.

But at the same time, our strategic resources are being handed over to foreign corporations through opaque processes, our youth are being co-opted as ambassadors of dependent models, and our capacity for autonomous development is being systematically undermined by lobbies that profit from our peripheral condition.

COP30 could be the moment to affirm **Amazonian sovereignty** and **Brazilian bioeconomy**. Instead, it risks becoming just another window to present our natural riches to foreign investors ready to be exploited.

#### The Choice Before Us

Brazilian bamboo could be one of the keys to our economic independence and environmental sustainability. With proper management, a single hectare of bamboo can:

- Sequester up to 80 tons of CO2/ha per year.
- Produce renewable raw material indefinitely (harvest every 3–5 years).
- Generate income for family farmers.
- Replace wood, reducing pressure on native forests.
- Create innovative products with properties superior to conventional materials.

But this will only happen if we **resist the neocolonial model** and build a sovereign national strategy.

We must:

**Denounce opaque processes** like the selection for the China course, demanding total transparency and investigation by control bodies.

**Value and include masters and pioneers** who possess practical knowledge fundamental for any genuine development of the sector.

**Democratize public policies**, preventing lobbies from capturing State instruments for their own benefit.

**Invest in national research and development**, creating own technologies adapted to our species and realities.

**Form balanced international partnerships**, where technology transfer is genuine and bilateral, not disguised subordination.

**Support family farmers and traditional communities**, who are the true guardians of biodiversity and ancestral knowledge.

**Build inclusive production chains**, adding value locally and distributing benefits fairly.

Brazil is at a crossroads. We can choose to be **protagonists** of the world bioeconomy, developing our potential in a sovereign and sustainable way. Or we can accept, once again, the **role of raw material suppliers** and **consumer market** for foreign products, now with the refinement of having our own youth promoting this dependency on social media.

COP30 will be the global stage where this choice becomes visible to the whole world. **Will we show sovereignty or subordination?**

Brazilian bamboo can set us free. But only if we have the courage to question who really benefits from the "cooperation" models presented to us as inevitable.

**The standing forest can be more valuable than felled. But only if it's us, Brazilians, who control and benefit from this wealth.**
